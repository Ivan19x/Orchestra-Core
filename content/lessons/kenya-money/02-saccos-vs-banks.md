# SACCOs vs banks — which is better for you?

Both SACCOs and banks let you save and borrow money — but they're built on fundamentally different models, and that difference shows up in dividends, loan terms, and how easily you can get your money out.

## The core difference: ownership

A **bank** is a company. When you deposit money, you're a customer — the bank's profits go to its shareholders, who are generally not you (unless you happen to also own bank shares).

A **SACCO (Savings and Credit Co-operative Organisation)** is a co-operative, owned by its members. When you deposit (technically, your deposits are often structured partly as "shares"), you're a part-owner. Profits the SACCO makes are distributed back to members as **dividends**, in proportion to their savings/shares — so members are, in a sense, both customers and shareholders at once.

This single difference in ownership explains most of the practical differences below.

## Returns on your savings

Bank savings accounts typically offer modest interest rates on regular savings — often quite low in real terms once inflation is considered.

SACCOs often pay annual **dividends** on members' deposits that are meaningfully higher than typical bank savings interest — sometimes in the double digits, depending on the SACCO's performance that year. This is one of the most commonly cited reasons people join SACCOs: the same money, sitting in savings, tends to grow faster.

The tradeoff: bank interest is typically credited regularly (e.g., monthly), while SACCO dividends are usually declared and paid **once a year**, after the SACCO's annual general meeting (AGM) — so the "return" is real, but it arrives on a different schedule, and isn't guaranteed in advance the way a bank's advertised rate is (it depends on the SACCO's actual performance that year).

## Borrowing: the multiplier effect

This is where SACCOs often stand out most. Many SACCOs let members borrow a **multiple of their savings/deposits** — for example, a member with KES 100,000 saved might be eligible to borrow 3x or 4x that amount (the exact multiplier varies by SACCO and loan product), often at interest rates lower than typical bank personal loans, with repayment terms designed around salaried members' pay cycles.

Banks, by contrast, generally assess loan eligibility through credit scoring, income verification, and collateral — a process that can be more flexible in *what* it considers, but is often less generous in *how much* relative to your existing savings, especially for members without a long credit history.

## Liquidity: getting your money out

This is the area where banks generally have the edge. Bank accounts (beyond fixed deposits) are typically accessible on demand — withdraw anytime, often instantly via ATM, agent, or mobile banking.

SACCO deposits/shares are often less liquid — withdrawing your savings (as opposed to just the dividend) may involve a formal process, sometimes with notice periods, and withdrawing your full "share capital" might affect your loan eligibility or even your membership status, depending on the SACCO's rules.

## Regulation: who's watching

Banks in Kenya are regulated by the **Central Bank of Kenya (CBK)**. SACCOs that take deposits are regulated by the **Sacco Societies Regulatory Authority (SASRA)**. Both are real regulatory regimes with licensing and oversight requirements — but they're different bodies with different rules, which is worth knowing if you ever need to raise a concern about either type of institution.

## Two broad types of SACCOs

- **Employer-based / workplace SACCOs** — membership tied to working at a particular organization or in a particular sector (common among teachers, civil servants, university staff, etc.). Contributions are often deducted directly from payroll.
- **Community-based SACCOs** — open membership, often organized around a geographic area, a common interest group, or a broader professional category, not tied to a single employer.

## So which is better?

For most people, the honest answer is **both, doing different jobs**:

- A **bank account** for day-to-day liquidity — the money you need access to without delay, and for services (cards, international transfers, business banking) that SACCOs typically don't provide.
- A **SACCO** for medium-to-long-term savings and as a borrowing tool — money you're disciplined about not touching often, that grows faster via dividends, and that gives you access to better loan terms when you need them.
