# T-Bills via CBK DhowCSD

> **Before we start:** this lesson explains what Treasury Bills are and how to access them — it's not a suggestion that T-Bills are the right place for your money. Where to keep savings depends on your own goals, how soon you might need the money, and your overall plan (see Money Basics, Module 4 on emergency funds).

## What a Treasury Bill actually is

When the Kenyan government needs to borrow money for the short term, it issues **Treasury Bills (T-Bills)** — essentially, an IOU from the government to whoever buys it. T-Bills come in three standard tenors: **91-day, 182-day, and 364-day** (roughly 3, 6, and 12 months).

Because the government is the borrower, T-Bills are generally considered among the lowest-**default**-risk investments available domestically — the government is highly unlikely to simply fail to pay. (This doesn't mean "no risk at all" — more on that below.)

## How the return works: it's a discount, not "interest" in the usual sense

T-Bills don't pay periodic interest the way a savings account does. Instead, they work on a **discount** basis: you pay *less* than the face value upfront, and receive the *full face value* when the bill matures. The difference is your return.

For example (illustrative numbers only): if a 91-day T-Bill has a face value of KES 100,000 and you buy it at a discount rate that results in a purchase price of KES 97,500, you'd receive KES 100,000 at maturity — a gain of KES 2,500 over 91 days on your KES 97,500 outlay.

To compare this to an annual interest rate (so you can compare it to, say, a SACCO dividend or savings account rate), the discount is annualized — this is the "yield" you'll see quoted. The actual discount rate and resulting yield are determined at auction, based on demand from all bidders — they change from auction to auction depending on market conditions, so there's no fixed number to memorize.

## How to access T-Bills directly: DhowCSD

Historically, buying T-Bills directly required going through a commercial bank as an agent. The Central Bank of Kenya's **DhowCSD** platform (dhowcsd.centralbank.go.ke) allows individuals to register and bid for government securities — including T-Bills and Treasury Bonds — directly, without a bank intermediary.

The general process:

1. **Register** on the DhowCSD platform, which involves providing identification details and linking a bank account (for both funding purchases and receiving proceeds at maturity).
2. **Place a bid** during an auction window — auctions happen on a regular schedule (typically weekly for T-Bills), announced in advance.
3. **Choose a bid type** — broadly, a "competitive" bid (you specify the rate you're willing to accept, with the risk it's rejected if too far from the eventually accepted rate) or a "non-competitive" bid (you accept whatever the weighted-average rate from the auction turns out to be — generally simpler for individual investors who aren't trying to optimize the rate).
4. **Funds are debited** from your linked account for successful bids, and the face value is credited back at maturity.

There's a **minimum investment amount** for T-Bills via DhowCSD, which has historically been set in increments (e.g., minimums around KES 50,000-100,000 depending on the instrument) — but minimums and rules can be updated by CBK, so checking the current requirements on the platform itself before assuming a figure is the reliable approach.

## The risk that *does* exist: it's not "zero risk"

"Government securities are low risk" usually refers specifically to **default risk** — the government paying you back. But two other things are worth understanding:

- **Inflation risk** — if inflation is running higher than your T-Bill's yield, your money is growing in shilling terms but losing purchasing power in real terms. A T-Bill yielding 10% when inflation is 8% has a "real" return closer to 2% — still positive, but much smaller than the headline number suggests.
- **Liquidity** — your money is committed for the bill's tenor (91/182/364 days). While there is a secondary market where government securities can sometimes be sold before maturity, this isn't as instant or guaranteed as withdrawing from a savings account.

## How T-Bills compare to SACCOs and money market funds

All three are ways of putting savings to work with relatively low risk, but with different tradeoffs:

- **T-Bills (via DhowCSD)**: direct exposure to government rates, fixed term, minimum investment requirement, return known at purchase (as a discount), no intermediary fees beyond the platform itself.
- **SACCO deposits**: dividends often competitive or higher, but paid annually and dependent on the SACCO's performance — not fixed or guaranteed in advance like a T-Bill's discount rate.
- **Money market funds (MMFs)**: professionally managed pools that often invest in a mix of T-Bills, bonds, and bank deposits — lower minimums than direct T-Bills, more liquidity (often a few days to withdraw), but with a management fee, and returns that fluctuate with the underlying portfolio.

None of these is universally "better" — they suit different combinations of amount available, time horizon, and how much hands-on management someone wants.
