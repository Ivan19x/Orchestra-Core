# Buying NSE shares step-by-step

> **Before we start:** this lesson explains the *mechanics* of how share-buying works on the Nairobi Securities Exchange (NSE) — the accounts, steps, and costs involved. It does not recommend any specific company, broker, or platform, and isn't a suggestion that buying shares is right for you. Whether and what to invest in depends on your own goals, timeline, and circumstances — and ideally, a solid emergency fund (Money Basics, Module 4) before any money goes toward shares at all.

## The big picture: what you're actually doing

Buying a share means buying a small ownership stake in a company listed on the NSE — companies across sectors like banking, telecommunications, manufacturing, and agriculture are listed there. Like most modern stock exchanges, shares are held electronically, not as physical paper certificates — which is where the CDS comes in.

## Step 1: Understand the CDS

The **Central Depository System (CDS)**, operated by the Central Depository & Settlement Corporation (CDSC), is the electronic system that records who owns what shares. To buy NSE shares, you need a **CDS account** — sometimes called a CDS number — which is essentially your personal electronic "vault" where your shares are recorded as belonging to you.

You don't open a CDS account directly with CDSC yourself in the typical retail process — it's opened *for* you through a licensed intermediary, which brings us to step 2.

## Step 2: Choose a licensed stockbroker or investment bank

To trade on the NSE, you go through a **licensed stockbroker or investment bank** — these are the firms authorized to execute trades on the exchange on your behalf. Several licensed firms in Kenya offer this service, including some with mobile apps that have made the process significantly more accessible than it used to be (historically, opening an account often meant visiting a physical office with paperwork).

When choosing, things worth comparing (without this lesson recommending any specific one) include: the account-opening process (online vs. in-person), minimum amounts if any, the trading platform's usability, and the fee structure (step 5).

## Step 3: Account opening and KYC

Opening a brokerage account involves standard "Know Your Customer" (KYC) requirements — generally a national ID or passport, KRA PIN, passport-sized photo, and proof of address. This is the same kind of documentation required to open a bank account, and exists to comply with anti-money-laundering regulations across the financial sector.

Once approved, your CDS account is opened as part of this process, linked to your broker.

## Step 4: Fund your account

Once your account is set up, you transfer money into it — typically via bank transfer or, increasingly, M-Pesa, depending on the broker's platform. This money sits in your trading account, ready to be used to buy shares.

## Step 5: Understand the costs

Buying and selling shares isn't free — there are a few layers of cost:

- **Brokerage commission** — a fee charged by your broker for executing the trade, often a percentage of the transaction value (sometimes with a minimum amount).
- **CDS/CDSC fees** — small fees related to the depository system itself.
- **NSE and regulatory fees** — small additional charges that fund the exchange and capital markets regulation.

These add up to a small percentage of each trade — meaning very small, frequent trades can see a meaningful chunk of the amount go to fees rather than the actual shares. This is one reason why a longer-term, less-frequent approach to buying shares is generally more cost-efficient than frequent small trades — separate from any view on which shares to hold.

## Step 6: Placing an order

With a funded account, you place an order through your broker's app or platform (or by phone/in-person with some brokers), specifying the company (by its ticker/code), the number of shares, and the price you're willing to pay (or "market price" to transact at the current available price). If a matching order exists on the other side, the trade executes — subject to the exchange's trading hours and rules.

## What happens after

Once a trade settles, the shares appear in your CDS account, linked to you. If the company pays **dividends** (distributions of profit to shareholders), these are typically paid directly to you — often to the bank or mobile money account linked to your brokerage account — based on how many shares you hold on the relevant date.

## The honest summary

The *mechanics* — CDS account, licensed broker, KYC, funding, costs, placing orders — are genuinely accessible to an everyday saver in Kenya, more so than a decade ago thanks to mobile-first brokers. The *harder* part — deciding what, when, and how much to buy, and how that fits into your broader financial picture — is a different question entirely, one this lesson deliberately doesn't answer, because the honest answer is "it depends on you," not "it depends on the stock."
