# M-Pesa fees demystified

M-Pesa is so woven into daily life in Kenya that it's easy to stop noticing its fees entirely — they're just small numbers that disappear from a balance. But over a month, those small numbers add up to a real expense, and understanding the *shape* of how they work can help you route money more cheaply without much extra effort.

## The core idea: tiered pricing

Almost every M-Pesa transaction type uses **tiered pricing** — the fee depends on which "band" your amount falls into, not a flat percentage. Sending KES 100 and sending KES 99 might cost the same, because they're in the same tier, while sending KES 101 could jump to the next tier's fee. This means amounts that sit just above a tier boundary are often quietly more expensive *per shilling* than amounts just below it.

The exact tiers and fees change from time to time (Safaricom periodically revises them, and there have been periods of fee waivers for certain transaction types). Rather than memorizing numbers that may be outdated by the time you read this, the more durable habit is: **check the fee shown on the confirmation screen before you confirm** — it's almost always displayed, and a moment's pause here is free.

## Why different transaction types cost differently

- **Send Money (person to person)** — generally has its own fee tier structure.
- **Withdraw (cash out at an agent)** — typically has a different, often higher, fee structure than sending — agents need to be compensated for providing physical cash.
- **Pay Bill** — used for paying businesses, utilities, and institutions with a business number. Fees here are often lower than Withdraw, and at various times certain Pay Bill transactions (like utility payments) have been fee-free or reduced.
- **Buy Goods (Till Number)** — used for in-person retail payments. Historically, Buy Goods transactions have often been free or very low-cost for the customer, which is part of why many small businesses prefer customers pay via Till rather than Send Money.
- **M-Pesa to Bank / Bank to M-Pesa** — has its own fee structure, generally separate from person-to-person transfers.

The practical takeaway: **the same amount of money, moved the same distance (person to person, roughly), can cost different amounts depending on which "rail" you use.** If a shop offers both a Till number and a personal phone number to pay, the Till option is often cheaper for you — worth a glance before paying.

## A simple habit: route, don't just send

Before moving money, a quick mental check — "is there a cheaper *type* of transaction for what I'm actually doing?" — can save real money over a year:

- Paying a shop? Buy Goods (Till) is often cheaper than Send Money to a personal number.
- Paying a bill (electricity, water, school fees)? Pay Bill, not Send Money to someone's personal number who then pays on your behalf.
- Moving your own money to savings? Many banks and SACCOs have direct M-Pesa integration that may cost less than withdraw-then-deposit.

## Fuliza — what it actually is

Fuliza is M-Pesa's **overdraft facility** — if you try to send money or pay for something and your M-Pesa balance is insufficient, Fuliza can cover the shortfall, *if* you've opted in and are eligible. The transaction goes through, and the amount you borrowed (plus a fee) is deducted automatically the next time money lands in your M-Pesa account.

The convenience is real — a transaction that would otherwise fail goes through instead. But it's still a loan, with a cost attached, and because repayment happens automatically and immediately when money arrives, it's easy to lose track of how often it's being used. If a meaningful chunk of your incoming money is routinely going straight to clearing a Fuliza balance, that's worth treating as a budget line item (back to Module 1) rather than background noise.
