# Good debt vs bad debt

"Avoid debt" is advice you'll hear constantly. It's also wrong — or at least incomplete.

The wealthiest people on the planet use debt constantly and deliberately. They borrow to buy property. They take loans to start businesses. They use credit to fund investments that return more than the interest they pay. Debt, used correctly, is a tool that multiplies your ability to build wealth.

The problem isn't debt. It's bad debt. And knowing the difference is one of the most important financial skills you can develop.

## Good debt: borrowing to create more than you borrow

Good debt has two characteristics. The cost of borrowing (the interest rate) is lower than the return you expect from what you're buying. And what you're buying either appreciates in value or generates income.

**A mortgage on a property** is the classic example. You borrow KES 4 million at 13% from a bank to buy a house. You rent it out for KES 40,000 a month — KES 480,000 a year. Your mortgage repayments are roughly KES 45,000 a month — KES 540,000 a year. You're paying slightly more than you earn in rent initially, but the property is also appreciating — in Nairobi, property values have historically grown 7-12% annually. Over 15-20 years, you own an asset worth multiples of what you paid, funded largely by rent and a loan. That's good debt working.

**A student loan for a marketable qualification** can be good debt. You borrow to fund education that increases your earning capacity by more than the interest costs over time. A HELB loan at relatively low interest funding a degree that adds KES 20,000/month to your income for 40 years is good debt.

**A business loan to expand a profitable operation** — borrowing KES 500,000 at 18% to buy inventory that generates KES 150,000 profit per cycle — is good debt if the maths works out.

## Bad debt: borrowing to consume

Bad debt funds things that lose value immediately and generate no return — and typically at very high interest rates.

**Mobile loans for daily expenses.** Borrowing on Fuliza to buy food, pay rent, or cover shortfalls is bad debt. The interest rate is extremely high when annualised, and what you're buying disappears immediately. You're paying more for something that's already gone.

**Buy-now-pay-later for electronics or clothes.** Your new phone loses 30% of its value the moment you leave the shop. Paying 20%+ interest on a depreciating asset doubles the damage.

**Credit to fund a lifestyle you can't afford.** Borrowing to maintain appearances — the car you can't afford, the holiday that empties your account, the outfit for someone's wedding — is the most destructive form of bad debt. The social pressure to spend beyond your means is real in Kenya, and so are the consequences.

## The grey area: car loans

Car loans are interesting because they sit between good and bad depending on circumstance.

A car depreciates — it loses value immediately and continuously. In that sense, a car loan is bad debt. But if the car enables you to earn more — a boda boda loan for a business, a vehicle needed to access a better-paying job that public transport can't reach, or a commercial vehicle for business — then the income generated can justify the cost. The test: does owning this car make you more money than the loan costs you?

If the answer is yes, it's potentially acceptable debt. If it's a lifestyle car funded by debt just to have one, it's bad debt wrapped in monthly payments.

## How to evaluate any debt

Before taking any loan, ask four questions.

What is the true annual interest rate? Mobile loans often advertise daily rates — 1.5% per day sounds small. Compounded annually, it's over 700%. Always annualise the rate before agreeing.

What am I buying with this money? Does it appreciate, generate income, or immediately lose value and disappear?

Is the return on what I'm buying higher than the interest I'm paying? If you're borrowing at 15% and the investment returns 25%, the maths works. If you're borrowing at 20% for something that returns nothing, you're losing 20% a year.

Can I genuinely service this debt without damaging other parts of my financial life? Debt that eats into your savings, emergency fund, or family obligations isn't good debt regardless of what you buy with it.

## Key takeaways

- Good debt buys things that appreciate or generate income at a rate higher than the interest you pay.
- Bad debt funds consumption — things that lose value immediately and generate no return.
- Mortgages, business loans, and education loans can be good debt. Mobile loans for daily expenses almost never are.
- Always annualise interest rates — 1.5% per day is over 700% annually.
- Before any loan, ask: what does this buy me, and does the return beat the interest?
