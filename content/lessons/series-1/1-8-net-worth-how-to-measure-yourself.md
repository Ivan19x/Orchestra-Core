# Net worth — how to measure yourself

Most people measure their financial life by their salary. "I earn KES 80,000 a month" feels like a statement of financial health. But salary tells you almost nothing useful. It tells you the size of the pipe, not the size of the tank.

Net worth tells you where you actually stand. It's the single number that captures your true financial position — and tracking it over time is the most honest feedback loop you can have.

## The formula

Net worth = Everything you own (assets) minus everything you owe (liabilities).

That's it. Two lists. Subtract one from the other.

If you own KES 150,000 in a money market fund, KES 50,000 in SACCO shares, and a laptop worth KES 80,000, your assets total KES 280,000.

If you owe KES 30,000 on a mobile loan, KES 15,000 to a friend, and KES 45,000 in a SACCO loan, your liabilities total KES 90,000.

Net worth: KES 280,000 − KES 90,000 = **KES 190,000.**

## What counts as an asset

Assets are things of financial value you own.

**Liquid assets**: money market funds, savings accounts, M-Pesa balance, cash.
**Investment assets**: shares on the NSE, unit trust holdings, Treasury bills you hold.
**SACCO shares and deposits**: the accumulated shares in your SACCO, not the loan facility.
**Property**: if you own land or a house, its estimated current market value.
**Business assets**: the value of inventory, equipment, or receivables if you run a business.

Note what's not usually on the list: your personal phone, clothes, furniture, and most everyday possessions. They have value but negligible resale value and aren't financial assets. Include them only if they're genuinely significant and realisable.

## What counts as a liability

Liabilities are everything you owe to anyone.

**Mobile loans**: Fuliza, KCB M-Pesa, branch loans, Tala, any outstanding balance.
**Bank loans**: outstanding loan balances, credit cards.
**SACCO loans**: borrowed against your SACCO shares.
**Chama debt**: if your chama has given you money you haven't contributed to yet.
**Informal debt**: money owed to family, friends, anyone.

Be honest. People consistently undercount their liabilities because informal debt feels different from bank debt. It isn't. It's still money you owe.

## What a healthy trajectory looks like

Nobody expects a 22-year-old to have a positive net worth. Student loans, zero savings, a borrowed first deposit — starting life with a negative net worth is normal.

What matters is the direction. Is your net worth going up month after month, year after year? That is the only financial question worth asking consistently.

A salary earner who earns KES 60,000 but spends everything and saves nothing has a stagnant net worth. An earner on KES 35,000 who saves 20% monthly and avoids debt is growing their net worth steadily. In 10 years, the second person is almost certainly in a stronger position despite earning less.

Track your net worth every six months. Write it in a notebook, a spreadsheet, a notes app. Six months of data shows a direction. Three years of data shows a trajectory. That trajectory is your real financial story — not your salary, not your job title, not what you appear to have.

## The net worth that lies

One important caveat: net worth can be inflated by assets that are hard to value or unlikely to be realised.

If you list your share of your family's upcountry land at a high estimated value, but that land has never been surveyed, has unclear title, and could never practically be sold, it's not really an asset in the meaningful sense. Count assets at realistic, realisable values.

Similarly, cars depreciate fast. A car bought for KES 2.5 million in 2022 may be worth KES 1.4 million today. List it at today's value, not what you paid.

## Key takeaways

- Net worth = assets minus liabilities. It's the single most important financial number you own.
- Salary measures income flow. Net worth measures accumulated wealth. They're completely different things.
- Track net worth every six months. Direction matters more than the current number.
- Be honest about liabilities — including informal debt to family and friends.
- Value assets realistically, not optimistically.
