# Inflation — why your KES 1,000 shrinks

Think about what KES 1,000 bought your parents in 2000. A full week of groceries. Today that same KES 1,000 buys maybe a day's worth of basics. The note looks identical. The number is the same. But the purchasing power has quietly, steadily evaporated.

This is inflation. And it's the most important economic force most people never properly understand.

## What inflation actually is

Inflation is the general rise in prices across an economy over time. Not one thing getting more expensive — everything. Bread, rent, matatu fares, school fees, airtime. When the average price level rises by 8% in a year, that's 8% inflation.

The most common measure is the Consumer Price Index (CPI) — a basket of goods and services that represents typical household spending. The Kenya National Bureau of Statistics (KNBS) tracks this monthly. When the CPI rises, prices are rising. Kenya has experienced inflation rates ranging from around 3% to over 10% in recent years depending on global oil prices, food supply, and the exchange rate.

## What causes inflation

Several things push prices up, and understanding the causes helps you anticipate them.

**Too much money chasing too few goods.** When the government prints money or banks create too much credit, more money exists but the same amount of goods does. Sellers can charge more because buyers have more money available. Prices rise.

**Import costs rising.** Kenya imports a lot — oil, machinery, some food. When the KES weakens against the dollar, everything imported becomes more expensive in shilling terms. A weaker KES means higher petrol prices, which means higher transport costs for every good in the country. The 2022-2023 period saw significant inflation in Kenya partly for this reason.

**Supply shocks.** When the supply of something essential drops suddenly — a drought cutting food production, a global shipping disruption — prices spike even if nothing changed with the money supply. Covid-19 created supply shocks globally, and Kenya felt them.

**Demand pull.** When an economy grows strongly and everyone has more money to spend, demand for goods and services rises faster than supply can match. Prices are pulled upward.

## How inflation affects your money specifically

If you have KES 100,000 sitting in a standard bank account earning 3% annual interest, and inflation is 7%, your real return is negative 4%. The number in your account grows slightly. But what it can buy shrinks. You're losing ground while feeling like you're standing still.

This is called **negative real returns** — when the nominal (stated) interest rate is lower than inflation. It's the silent tax on savers who park money in low-yield accounts.

The mathematics are unforgiving over time. At 7% annual inflation, prices double in roughly 10 years. Something that costs KES 5,000 today costs KES 10,000 in 2036. Your savings need to at least keep pace with inflation to maintain their value — and ideally beat it to actually grow.

## What beats inflation in Kenya

Cash savings accounts at most Kenyan banks earn 3-7% interest. With inflation often in that same range, you're barely treading water.

Money market funds have historically returned 10-14% in Kenya — above inflation in most years. Your money grows in real terms. Government bonds and Treasury bills offer similar or slightly lower returns with government-backed security.

NSE equities are volatile but have historically returned more than inflation over long periods. Property — again over long periods — has also beaten inflation in most Nairobi locations.

The pattern is consistent: assets that produce income or appreciate tend to beat inflation. Cash sitting still tends to lose to it.

## The positive side: inflation and debt

There is one group that actually benefits from inflation: borrowers with fixed-rate debt.

If you took a mortgage at 14% fixed for 20 years and inflation runs at 10% for the next decade, the real cost of your debt is only 4%. Your salary rises with inflation. Your fixed mortgage payment stays the same. In real terms, your debt is shrinking while your income grows. This is why property investors often like a moderate amount of inflation — it erodes the real value of their mortgage while their asset value and rental income rises.

This is also why governments sometimes find inflation convenient. National debt becomes easier to service when inflation is running high and tax revenues are rising with inflation even though the debt face value stays fixed.

## Key takeaways

- Inflation is the general rise in prices over time. At 7%, prices double every 10 years.
- Cash savings earning less than inflation lose real purchasing power silently every year.
- The CBK manages inflation through interest rates — raising rates to cool inflation, cutting them to stimulate growth.
- Money market funds (10-14% returns) have historically beaten Kenya's inflation. Basic savings accounts often don't.
- Borrowers with fixed-rate debt can benefit from inflation as it erodes the real value of what they owe.
