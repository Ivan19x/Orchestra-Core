# How banks create money

Most people think banks are like giant safes. You deposit KES 10,000. They store it. Somebody else comes along and borrows some of it. Simple.

That's not what happens. Banks create money out of thin air — legally, deliberately, and constantly. Understanding this mechanic is one of the most important things you can know about how the financial world actually works.

## The vault that's mostly empty

When you deposit KES 10,000 at Equity Bank, Equity doesn't lock that money away for you. They keep a small fraction — maybe 10-20% — as a reserve, in case you and other depositors want to withdraw. The rest they lend out to someone else.

So your KES 10,000 deposit becomes KES 8,000 lent to a small business owner who needed working capital. That business owner deposits the KES 8,000 into their account at KCB. KCB keeps a reserve and lends out, say, KES 6,400 to someone who needs a car loan. That person's seller deposits KES 6,400 into Co-operative Bank. And it continues.

Your original KES 10,000 has, through this chain, become roughly KES 35,000-40,000 of money in circulation. Nobody printed extra notes. The central bank didn't authorise anything new. The bank system simply lent and re-lent, and each cycle created a new deposit.

This is called **fractional reserve banking** — banks only hold a fraction of deposits as actual reserves. The rest is lent into existence as new money.

## Why banks can do this

You might wonder: is this legal? Is it ethical? The answer is yes to the first question and contested on the second, but it's been the standard model globally for centuries.

It works as long as not everyone tries to withdraw at the same time. Your KES 10,000 is "there" in the sense that you can withdraw it. But it's also powering KES 30,000+ of economic activity simultaneously. If every depositor at a bank tried to withdraw on the same day — a "bank run" — the bank collapses, because the vault is mostly empty.

This is why governments insure bank deposits (in Kenya, up to KES 500,000 per account via the Kenya Deposit Insurance Corporation). The insurance exists because without it, the fractional reserve system would be perpetually vulnerable to panics.

## The Central Bank of Kenya's role

The CBK sits above this whole system. It sets the reserve requirement — how much commercial banks must hold back. It sets the base interest rate — the rate at which banks borrow from the CBK itself when they need reserves. When the CBK raises interest rates, borrowing becomes more expensive, banks lend less, and less new money enters the economy. When it cuts rates, borrowing is cheaper, banks lend more, and more money circulates.

This is how monetary policy works. The CBK doesn't need to print money to stimulate the economy. It can simply lower interest rates and watch banks multiply existing deposits into more loans, more spending, and more economic activity.

When you hear "the CBK raised rates," it means: credit is getting more expensive, loans will shrink, less money will be created by banks, and the economy will slow slightly. Usually this is done to fight inflation. When prices are rising too fast, reducing the money supply slows spending down and pulls prices back.

## What this means for you

Three things directly affect your life from this understanding.

First, **interest rates on your loans and savings are connected to this system**. When the CBK raises rates, your mobile loan interest goes up. Your mortgage repayment goes up. Your savings account interest also goes up — though banks are much faster to raise what you pay than what you earn.

Second, **keeping money in a bank is not the same as keeping it safe**. Your deposit is partially lent out immediately. The bank owes you KES 10,000 but doesn't have KES 10,000 sitting in a vault. It has the promise of KES 10,000 — backed by the assets of the bank and the government's deposit insurance. That's usually fine. But it's not zero risk.

Third, **this is why "tight money" and "loose money" matter**. When credit is easy, businesses borrow, expand, hire, and the economy grows. When credit tightens, growth slows. Every time you see a headline about CBK interest rate decisions, this is what's really being decided: how much new money will the banking system create this month?

## Key takeaways

- Banks don't store your full deposit — they lend most of it out, creating new money in the process.
- This is fractional reserve banking and it's legal, standard, and global.
- Your KES 10,000 deposit can power KES 30,000-40,000+ of economic activity across multiple banks.
- The CBK controls how aggressively this happens by setting interest rates and reserve requirements.
- Kenya Deposit Insurance Corporation covers up to KES 500,000 per account if a bank fails.
