# Compound interest — the eighth wonder

Albert Einstein allegedly called compound interest the eighth wonder of the world. Whether he actually said it or not, the idea is correct. Compound interest is the closest thing to a financial superpower that an ordinary person can access — and almost nobody starts using it early enough.

Here's the concept in one sentence: **you earn interest on your interest, not just your original money.** That feedback loop, given enough time, produces numbers that feel impossible.

## The numbers that change minds

Let's say two people, Amina and Baraka, both want to retire comfortably. Both earn reasonable Nairobi salaries. The only difference is when they start saving.

Amina starts at 22. She puts KES 5,000 a month into a money market fund earning 12% annually. At 35 — just 13 years later — she stops contributing entirely. She puts in nothing more for the rest of her working life. Total contributed: KES 780,000.

Baraka starts at 35. He also puts KES 5,000 a month into the same fund at the same 12% rate. He keeps going until he's 65 — 30 full years of contributions. Total contributed: KES 1,800,000.

At 65, who has more?

Amina: **approximately KES 24 million.**
Baraka: **approximately KES 17 million.**

Amina contributed less than half of what Baraka did. She stopped saving 30 years before he did. She still ends up with more — by KES 7 million. The only thing she had that he didn't was a 13-year head start.

This is compound interest. Time is the ingredient that no amount of money can buy back.

## Why it works — the mechanics

Simple interest earns returns only on your original amount. If you deposit KES 100,000 at 10% simple interest, you earn KES 10,000 every year. After 10 years: KES 200,000. Straightforward.

Compound interest earns returns on your original amount plus all previously earned interest. Year 1: KES 100,000 earns KES 10,000 → total KES 110,000. Year 2: KES 110,000 earns KES 11,000 → total KES 121,000. Year 3: KES 121,000 earns KES 12,100 → total KES 133,100.

The growth accelerates. By year 20, your KES 100,000 has become KES 672,000 — without a single additional deposit. The money earned money, and that money earned more money.

Most Kenyan money market funds compound daily or monthly, which is even more powerful than annual compounding. The CIC Money Market Fund, Britam, Sanlam, and others have historically delivered 10-14% annual returns, though past returns don't guarantee future ones.

## The rule of 72

Here's a useful shortcut: divide 72 by your interest rate to estimate how many years it takes to double your money.

At 12% annual return: 72 ÷ 12 = **6 years to double your money.**

KES 100,000 at 22 → KES 200,000 at 28 → KES 400,000 at 34 → KES 800,000 at 40 → KES 1,600,000 at 46 → KES 3,200,000 at 52 → KES 6,400,000 at 58.

Starting with KES 100,000 at 22 and never adding another shilling, you can reach KES 6.4 million by 58 at 12% returns. That's the power of time. That's compound interest at work.

At 10%: money doubles every 7.2 years.
At 15%: money doubles every 4.8 years.

The higher the rate, the faster the doubling. But even modest rates produce extraordinary results with enough time.

## The enemy: debt compound interest

Compound interest cuts both ways. When it works for you as a saver, it's a superpower. When it works against you as a borrower, it's a trap.

Mobile loans in Kenya — Fuliza, KCB M-Pesa, and others — charge interest rates that can reach 60-100%+ annually when annualised. At 100% compound interest, a debt doubles in one year. A KES 10,000 loan at that rate becomes KES 20,000 owed in 12 months without repaying a shilling.

This is exactly what happens to people stuck in the mobile loan cycle. They borrow KES 2,000 for rent. They can't repay it plus interest on payday, so they borrow more to cover it. The debt compounds faster than their income grows, and the hole gets deeper every month.

Understanding compound interest means understanding both why to save early and why to destroy debt fast — especially high-interest debt. The mathematics is identical; only the direction changes.

## Starting with very little

You don't need KES 100,000 to start. Money market funds in Kenya accept as little as KES 1,000 to open. Ziidi (M-Pesa), Sanlam, ICEA Lion, and others are accessible via phone.

KES 500 a month from age 20, at 12% annually, becomes approximately KES 3.2 million by age 60. KES 500. The same cost as a lunch out. Every month for 40 years. KES 3.2 million.

Nobody is too poor to start. They might be too uninformed to start — and that's exactly what this lesson is for.

## Key takeaways

- Compound interest earns returns on your interest, not just your original money — producing exponential, not linear, growth.
- Time is the most powerful ingredient. Starting 10 years earlier beats contributing twice as much.
- The Rule of 72: divide 72 by your interest rate to find how many years to double your money.
- At 12%, money doubles every 6 years.
- Compound interest also works in reverse on debt — high-interest mobile loans compound against you just as fast.
- Even KES 500-1,000 a month invested at 20 can produce millions by retirement.
