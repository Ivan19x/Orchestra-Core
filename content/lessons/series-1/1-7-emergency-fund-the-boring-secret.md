# The emergency fund — the boring secret

Here's a situation most Kenyans know personally: an unexpected cost arrives — a medical bill, a job loss, a broken phone you need for work, a family emergency — and because there's no cushion anywhere, you borrow. You borrow at high interest from a mobile loan, from a friend, from a SACCO against your shares. The emergency passes but the debt remains. Next month is tighter. The cycle starts.

The emergency fund is the thing that breaks this cycle before it starts. It's the most boring, least glamorous financial move you can make. It's also the one that protects everything else.

## What an emergency fund is

An emergency fund is 3-6 months of your essential living expenses saved in a liquid, accessible account — not invested, not in a SACCO where withdrawal takes time, not tied up anywhere. Available within 24-48 hours when you need it.

Essential expenses means: rent, food, transport, utilities, and any commitments you cannot miss. Not wants. Not subscriptions. Just survival costs.

If your essential monthly expenses are KES 25,000, your emergency fund target is KES 75,000 to KES 150,000. That's it. Once you hit that number, this bucket is full. You stop adding to it and redirect that money to investments.

## Why liquid matters

Stocks can drop 30% in a month. A SACCO withdrawal often takes 1-3 months for substantial amounts. Chama savings require group agreement. Property can't be sold in a week.

When a genuine emergency hits — a job loss, a sudden medical cost, a critical household breakdown — you need money now, not in three months. The emergency fund lives in a money market fund (accessible in 24-48 hours with most Kenyan providers like Ziidi, CIC, or Sanlam), a savings account, or your M-Pesa. Somewhere liquid. Not in investments.

The point isn't to earn returns on this money. It's to make sure you never have to borrow at high interest rates during a crisis.

## Building it when you have nothing

Starting from zero with KES 25,000 expenses and no cushion at all, here's a realistic approach.

Target first: KES 25,000 — one month of expenses. Even one month changes your financial situation dramatically. A single month of cushion means you can handle most everyday emergencies without borrowing.

Save aggressively toward this first milestone. If necessary, temporarily drop wants spending to near zero. No eating out, no entertainment beyond free options. Three to four months of focused saving should get most employed people to one month's expenses.

Then breathe, restore some wants spending, and build toward three months at a slower pace.

## What counts as a real emergency

This sounds obvious, but for many people it isn't once the fund exists and temptation arrives.

Real emergencies: sudden medical treatment, unexpected job loss, essential home repair (burst pipe, no water), a required work tool that breaks.

Not emergencies: a sale on something you want, an event you feel social pressure to attend, impulse purchases, anything planned in advance. If you knew about it more than a month ago, it's not an emergency — it's poor planning.

Protect the fund fiercely. The moment it becomes a general savings pot it stops doing its job.

## The peace of mind dividend

There's a financial return on an emergency fund that doesn't show up in any calculation: the change in how you live.

When you have three months of expenses saved, the anxiety around money changes fundamentally. A bad month at work doesn't become a crisis. An unexpected bill doesn't spiral. You make better financial decisions generally because you're not operating from scarcity and panic.

People without emergency funds often make terrible financial decisions when emergencies hit — taking out high-interest loans, selling investments at the worst time, making impulsive choices under stress. The fund prevents all of that. Its value is partly financial and partly psychological, and both parts are real.

## Key takeaways

- An emergency fund is 3-6 months of essential living expenses in a liquid, accessible account.
- It lives in a money market fund or savings account — not tied up in investments or SACCO.
- The goal is not returns. The goal is avoiding high-interest debt during crises.
- Build to one month first, then three months, then six.
- Protect it: only genuine unexpected emergencies justify withdrawal, not desires or poor planning.
