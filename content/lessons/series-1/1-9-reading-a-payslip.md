# Reading a payslip line by line

For most people, the first payslip is a moment of pure confusion. A number you were promised — say KES 40,000 — turns into a different, smaller number in your account, and the payslip is full of acronyms that were never explained.

Let's fix that. Every payslip in Kenya follows roughly the same structure, even if the layout differs between employers.

## Gross pay vs. net pay

**Gross pay** is the headline number — your salary before anything is taken out. It's the number in your offer letter or employment contract.

**Net pay** (sometimes called "take-home") is what actually lands in your account, after all deductions. The gap between gross and net is where the acronyms live.

## The statutory deductions

These are deductions required by law — every employer in Kenya must apply them. The exact rates and bands change periodically (rates are set by government, not by your employer), so always check the current figures on your payslip or with KRA rather than memorizing numbers that may be out of date. But here's what each one *is*:

- **PAYE (Pay As You Earn)** — this is income tax, deducted directly from your salary by your employer and remitted to the Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA). It's calculated using tax bands — you pay a higher rate on higher portions of income, not on your whole salary at one rate. There's usually a personal relief that reduces the amount of tax you owe.
- **NSSF (National Social Security Fund)** — a retirement savings contribution. Both you and your employer contribute, and the money is meant to build up a pension pot for your retirement. Think of it as a forced, long-term savings account that you'll be glad exists decades from now.
- **SHIF (Social Health Insurance Fund)** — Kenya's health insurance contribution, which replaced the older NHIF. It funds access to healthcare services, again with both employee and sometimes employer contributions depending on the scheme rules in force.
- **Affordable Housing Levy** — a relatively newer deduction that goes toward a national housing fund, intended to support affordable housing development.

## The non-statutory deductions

These vary person to person, because they depend on choices you (or your employer on your behalf) have made:

- **SACCO contributions** — if you've joined a SACCO through your workplace, your monthly contribution is often deducted directly from payroll.
- **Loan repayments** — SACCO loans, salary advances, or staff loans are typically repaid via payroll deduction.
- **Union dues, insurance premiums, or other voluntary deductions** — depends on your employer and what you've opted into.

## How to actually read your payslip

A typical payslip has two columns: **earnings** (gross salary, plus any allowances like housing, transport, or hardship allowance) and **deductions** (everything listed above). Net pay = total earnings − total deductions. The math should add up — and it's worth checking it does, because payroll errors happen more often than people assume.

A useful habit: when you get a new job or a salary change, sit down with your first payslip and identify every single line item. If something is unfamiliar, ask HR what it is and why it's there. There's no such thing as a question too basic when it's about money that's legally yours.

## Why this matters beyond curiosity

Understanding your payslip isn't just about satisfying curiosity — it's the foundation for everything else in personal finance. Your budget (Module 3) should be built on **net pay**, not gross — a surprisingly common mistake. And your NSSF and SHIF contributions are assets you're building, even though they don't show up in your bank balance — they're part of your real financial picture, just on a longer timeline.

## Key takeaways

- Gross pay is the headline number; net pay is what actually lands in your account — budget off net, not gross.
- PAYE, NSSF, SHIF, and the Housing Levy are statutory — every employer must deduct them, and the rates are set by government, not negotiable per-employer.
- SACCO contributions, loan repayments, and union dues are non-statutory — they exist only if you've opted in.
- NSSF and SHIF contributions are still your money, just on a longer timeline — they're part of your real financial picture even though they're invisible in your day-to-day balance.
- Check the math on your own payslip every time you get one — payroll errors happen more often than people assume.
