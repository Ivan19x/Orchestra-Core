# The 50/30/20 rule

Budgeting sounds complicated. Spreadsheets, categories, tracking every mandazi. Most people try it for two weeks, give up, and go back to spending until the money runs out.

The 50/30/20 rule is different. It's not a spreadsheet. It's a single decision made once, applied to every paycheck automatically. It's so simple that even people who hate budgeting can follow it.

## The idea

Split every shilling you earn into three buckets — no more, no less.

**50% — Needs.** Half your income goes to things you cannot live without. Rent. Food. Transport to work. Electricity. Phone airtime. NHIF contributions. These are non-negotiable. If you earn KES 40,000 a month, KES 20,000 covers your survival.

**30% — Wants.** Three-tenths goes to things that improve your life but aren't survival. Eating out. New clothes beyond basics. Entertainment. A nicer phone than the one you technically need. DStv. These are real — everyone has wants, and pretending you don't only leads to binge spending later.

**20% — Savings and debt repayment.** The final fifth goes to your future self. SACCO contributions. Emergency fund. Investment. If you have mobile loans, credit card debt, or any outstanding balance, this bucket also services that debt — clearing it as fast as possible, because debt costs more than savings earns.

## What this looks like on a Kenyan salary

Let's make this concrete. You've just started a job in Nairobi earning a net salary of KES 55,000 a month after PAYE and NSSF deductions.

| Bucket | Percentage | Monthly amount |
|---|---|---|
| Needs | 50% | KES 27,500 |
| Wants | 30% | KES 16,500 |
| Savings / debt | 20% | KES 11,000 |

Your KES 27,500 needs bucket covers: rent in a decent Nairobi estate (KES 12,000-18,000), transport (KES 3,000-5,000 matatu or fuel), food basics (KES 6,000-8,000), electricity and water (KES 1,500-2,500), phone bundles (KES 500-1,000). It's tight but workable if you're not paying rent in Westlands.

Your KES 16,500 wants bucket is real money. That covers dinner out a few times a month, a streaming service, occasional shopping, and social life.

Your KES 11,000 savings bucket, invested consistently in a money market fund earning around 12% annually, becomes roughly KES 190,000 in 18 months and KES 440,000 in three years — without you doing anything except not touching it.

## The Kenya reality check

The 50/30/20 rule was designed for American incomes and American costs. In Kenya, some adjustments are honest and necessary.

If you earn KES 25,000 net in Nairobi, 50% needs is KES 12,500. Rent alone in most decent areas starts at KES 8,000-10,000. The math gets uncomfortable fast. In this case, reduce wants aggressively — perhaps 60/15/25 — and treat increasing income as urgent. The rule doesn't override reality; it gives you a target to aim toward.

If you're sending money home — parents, siblings, family obligations — that comes from your needs bucket, not your savings. Family obligations are not wants. They're real costs that have to be accounted for honestly, or the budget will break every month and you'll stop trying.

## How to actually implement this

Don't track. Automate.

The day your salary arrives, immediately move 20% to a separate M-Pesa wallet, SACCO account, or savings account. Before you buy anything. Before you pay rent. Move it first.

This is called **paying yourself first**, and it's the single most important habit in personal finance. Once it's in the savings account, it's psychologically gone. You budget the remaining 80% for needs and wants. Most people try to save what's left after spending — there's never anything left.

Set up a standing order with your bank or a recurring M-Pesa to SACCO transfer on the same day as your payroll date. Automation makes this frictionless.

## Key takeaways

- 50% of net income to needs, 30% to wants, 20% to savings and debt repayment.
- Paying yourself first — moving savings before anything else — is what makes this work in practice.
- For lower Nairobi salaries, adjust to 60/15/25 or 65/10/25 but never abandon the savings bucket entirely.
- Family obligations count as needs, not wants — budget them honestly.
- Automate the 20% transfer on payday so willpower isn't required.
