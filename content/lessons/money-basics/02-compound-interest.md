# What compound interest actually does

Here's a sentence that sounds boring but is actually one of the most useful things you'll ever learn about money: **interest on interest changes everything, given enough time.**

## Simple interest vs. compound interest

If you put KES 10,000 into something paying 10% **simple** interest per year, you earn KES 1,000 every year, forever, on that original KES 10,000. After 5 years: KES 15,000.

If it's 10% **compound** interest, the first year is the same — you earn KES 1,000, ending with KES 11,000. But the second year, you earn 10% on KES 11,000, not KES 10,000 — that's KES 1,100. The third year, 10% on KES 12,100. Each year's interest is calculated on a slightly bigger number than the last.

Over 5 years, compound interest gets you to roughly KES 16,105 — not a huge difference yet. But stretch it to 20 years, and simple interest gives you KES 30,000 while compound interest gives you roughly KES 67,275. The gap isn't linear — it accelerates.

## The "Rule of 72" — a mental shortcut

Want a quick way to estimate how long money takes to double at a given interest rate, without a calculator? Divide 72 by the interest rate.

- At 12% per year: 72 ÷ 12 = **6 years** to double.
- At 8% per year: 72 ÷ 8 = **9 years** to double.
- At 6% per year: 72 ÷ 6 = **12 years** to double.

It's an approximation, not exact, but it's close enough to be genuinely useful — especially for comparing two options quickly in your head.

## Why "starting early" beats "starting big"

Imagine two people. Person A saves KES 5,000 a month starting at age 25. Person B waits until 35 to start, but saves KES 8,000 a month to "catch up." Assuming similar returns, Person A — despite contributing less per month and starting with less discipline required — typically ends up ahead by retirement age. Those extra 10 years of compounding do more work than the extra KES 3,000 a month ever could.

This isn't an argument that it's "too late" if you're starting later — it's never too late, and something is always better than nothing. It's an argument for *starting*, even small, *now*, because time is the ingredient compounding needs most and it's the one ingredient you can't buy back later.

## The flip side: compounding works against borrowers too

Here's the part that doesn't get talked about enough: the exact same mechanic that quietly grows your SACCO deposits also quietly grows your debt — and debt usually compounds *faster* and over *shorter* periods.

Take a mobile loan — Fuliza, M-Shwari, Tala, or similar. These often charge fees on a daily or monthly basis. A "small" facility fee of a few percent might sound trivial on, say, KES 1,000. But if it's charged repeatedly — say you roll the balance over, or take a new loan to cover the last one — the effective annual rate can be dramatically higher than it looks at the moment you tap "borrow."

The danger isn't that any single mobile loan is catastrophic. It's that **the convenience hides the compounding.** Ten seconds to borrow KES 500 doesn't feel like a financial decision with a real cost — but the math behind it is the same math that, in the other direction, grows your retirement savings.

## The takeaway

Compound interest doesn't care whether it's working for you or against you — it just multiplies whatever balance it's attached to, over and over, on schedule. Your job is to notice which side of that equation you're on for any given account or loan, and to remember that **time is the multiplier** — for savings, that's an argument to start now; for debt, it's an argument to clear it fast.
