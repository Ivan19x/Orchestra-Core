# Emergency funds — how much and where

An emergency fund has one job: to make sure that when something unexpected happens — a medical bill, a job loss, an urgent family need, a broken phone you rely on for work — you're choosing how to respond, instead of being forced into a high-interest loan because there's no other option.

Without one, *every* emergency becomes a debt event. With one, most emergencies become inconvenient but manageable.

## "Three to six months of expenses" is a target, not a starting line

You've probably heard that an emergency fund should cover three to six months of essential expenses. For most people, hearing that number for the first time is discouraging — it can feel like a mountain that makes starting feel pointless.

Reframe it: that's the *target*, not the entry requirement. A more realistic progression looks like:

1. **First milestone: one month of essential expenses.** This alone covers a huge share of real-world emergencies — a delayed salary, an unexpected bill, a short gap between jobs.
2. **Second milestone: three months.** This starts covering bigger disruptions — a longer job search, a more serious medical situation.
3. **Stretch goal: six months.** This is genuinely for major life disruptions, and many people never fully reach it while also pursuing other goals — and that's fine. Even a partial fund changes your options dramatically compared to none.

The point isn't to hit six months immediately. It's to make sure that *some* buffer exists between you and the next mobile loan app.

## Where to keep it: the three-way tradeoff

Every place you could put emergency savings involves a tradeoff between three things: **liquidity** (how fast you can get the money), **return** (how much it grows while sitting there), and **friction** (how hard it is to spend impulsively).

- **M-Pesa wallet / current account.** Maximum liquidity, near-zero return, almost zero friction. Good for nothing except money you're about to spend — an emergency fund kept here tends to quietly become "fun money."
- **Locked savings products (e.g., M-Shwari Lock Savings, bank fixed deposits).** Lower liquidity (money is locked for a set period), modest return, high friction (which is the *point* — it's harder to dip into casually). Good for the portion of your fund you're confident you won't need on zero notice.
- **SACCO savings.** Often pays attractive annual dividends, and the friction of withdrawal (sometimes requiring notice or a withdrawal process) can help prevent impulsive spending. The tradeoff: SACCO dividends are usually paid annually and withdrawals aren't instant — so a SACCO account alone may not cover a same-day emergency.
- **Money market funds (MMFs)**, offered by various licensed fund managers, pool investor money into low-risk instruments like government securities and bank deposits. They typically offer better returns than a regular savings account, with withdrawals usually processed within a few business days — a middle ground between "instant" and "locked."

## A practical split

Many people find a blended approach works best: keep a small slice (enough for a true same-day emergency — say, a week's worth of essential expenses) somewhere instantly accessible, and put the bulk of the fund somewhere with a bit more friction and a bit more return — a locked savings product, SACCO, or money market fund.

The "friction" part matters more than people expect. An emergency fund that's too easy to access has a way of getting spent on non-emergencies — a sale, a slightly-too-expensive gift, a "treat yourself" moment that felt justified at the time. A little inconvenience is a feature, not a bug.

## Building it without a windfall

Most emergency funds aren't built from a bonus or lump sum — they're built the same way as any savings goal: a fixed amount, automatically set aside, every time money comes in (this connects directly back to the "pay yourself first" idea from Module 1). Even KES 1,000–2,000 a month adds up to a meaningful buffer within a year, and the goal compounds in importance the longer it exists — every month it's untouched is a month closer to genuinely changing how emergencies feel.
