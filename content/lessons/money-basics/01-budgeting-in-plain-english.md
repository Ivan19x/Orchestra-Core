# Budgeting in plain English

Say "budget" to most people and they hear "diet." A list of things you're not allowed to do anymore. No wonder most budgets survive about as long as a New Year's resolution.

Here's a more useful way to think about it: **a budget is just a plan for where your money goes, made before it goes there.** That's the whole idea. Without a plan, your money still goes somewhere — you just find out where in arrears, usually around day 20 of the month, when you're wondering where it all went.

## The order matters more than the amounts

Most people's money flows like this: salary lands → bills get paid → daily spending happens → whatever's left (if anything) gets "saved."

Flip the order and the whole thing changes: salary lands → savings/goals get set aside first → bills get paid → whatever's left is yours to spend freely, no guilt required.

This is sometimes called "pay yourself first." It sounds like a slogan, but it's really just sequencing. The money for your goals never sits in your spendable balance long enough to feel optional.

## A simple split that works in Kenya

A common starting framework is **50/30/20** — 50% needs, 30% wants, 20% savings/goals. The exact numbers matter less than having *some* split you check yourself against. Let's run it on a KES 30,000 monthly salary, after deductions:

- **Needs (≈50% → KES 15,000):** rent or rent contribution, electricity tokens, water, food staples, transport to work/school, airtime and data for essentials.
- **Wants (≈30% → KES 9,000):** eating out, entertainment, clothes beyond the basics, subscriptions, the extra data bundle for Netflix or TikTok.
- **Savings/goals (≈20% → KES 6,000):** SACCO contribution, emergency fund, a specific goal (rent deposit, a course, a phone upgrade).

If your numbers don't fit 50/30/20 — say rent alone eats 60% — that's not a personal failure, it's information. It tells you the lever to pull (cheaper housing, more income, or both) is bigger than tweaking your data bundle.

## The leaks nobody budgets for

A few categories quietly eat money in ways that don't show up until you look closely:

- **Transport** — matatu fares vary by time of day, weather, and fuel prices. A "KES 50 fare" can become KES 100 during rain, and that difference rarely gets planned for.
- **Small M-Pesa transactions** — sending KES 200 here and there feels free. The transaction costs aren't huge individually, but add them up over a month and they're a real line item.
- **"Soft" loans** — Fuliza, M-Shwari, Tala and similar apps make borrowing KES 500 take about ten seconds. Convenient, but each one carries a cost, and they're easy to forget you've taken until the deduction happens.

None of these are "bad." They're just invisible until you write them down — which is the entire point of a budget.

## A one-week experiment

You don't need an app or a spreadsheet to start. For one week, write down *everything* that leaves your account or wallet — M-Pesa, cash, card, everything — no matter how small. Don't change your behavior yet, just observe.

At the end of the week, group what you wrote into needs, wants, and savings. Most people find at least one surprise — often in transport, airtime, or small transfers. That surprise is the most useful output of the whole exercise. It's the thing your plan was missing.
