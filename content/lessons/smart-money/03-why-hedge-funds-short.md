# Why hedge funds short stocks

> **Before we start:** short-selling is a complex, high-risk strategy generally unsuitable for individual investors without significant experience and capital. This lesson explains the *concept* and why it's part of how markets work — not as something to try, but as something to understand when you encounter it in the news.

## What "shorting" means, step by step

Normally, investing follows a simple sequence: buy low, hope it goes higher, sell high. **Shorting reverses the order**: you sell first, hoping to buy back later at a lower price.

How do you sell something you don't own? You **borrow** it. Here's the sequence:

1. You borrow shares of a company from someone who owns them (usually arranged through a broker), agreeing to return identical shares later.
2. You immediately sell those borrowed shares at the current price.
3. Later, you buy the same number of shares back on the market — hopefully at a lower price than you sold for.
4. You return the borrowed shares to their owner.
5. The difference between your original sale price and your buy-back price (minus borrowing costs) is your profit — or loss.

If the price falls, you buy back cheaper than you sold — profit. If the price *rises*, you have to buy back at a higher price than you sold — a loss.

## Why funds do this — three legitimate reasons

### 1. Betting against overvaluation

Just as someone might believe a company is undervalued and buy it, someone might believe a company is significantly *overvalued* — perhaps its business is declining, or its accounting looks questionable — and short it, profiting if the market eventually agrees.

### 2. Hedging

A fund that owns a portfolio of companies in, say, the banking sector might short a broad index of bank stocks — not because it expects banks to crash, but to protect itself if the *whole sector* falls for reasons unrelated to its specific picks. The short position offsets some of that broader risk. This is "hedging" in the literal sense — reducing exposure to a risk you don't want, while keeping exposure to the risk (picking good individual companies) you do.

### 3. Market-neutral strategies

Some funds aim to make money regardless of whether markets go up or down overall, by being long (own) some stocks they think will do *relatively* well and short others they think will do *relatively* poorly — betting on the gap between the two, not on market direction.

## Why shorting is so much riskier than buying

When you buy a stock, the most you can lose is what you paid — the price can fall to zero, but no further. Your maximum loss is capped and known in advance.

When you short a stock, there's **no upper limit** on how high the price can go — in theory, it can rise indefinitely. Your potential loss is, in theory, unlimited. This asymmetry is the single biggest reason shorting is considered far riskier than buying, even when the underlying analysis is sound.

## The short squeeze — a case study in market psychology

A "short squeeze" happens when a stock that many investors have shorted starts rising sharply — for any reason. As the price rises, short-sellers face mounting losses, and at some point, many of them decide (or are forced) to "cover" — buy back shares to close their position and stop the bleeding. But buying back shares *adds buying pressure*, which pushes the price up further, which forces *more* short-sellers to cover, in a feedback loop.

A well-known real-world example occurred in early 2021 with a US retailer's stock, which had an unusually large number of shares shorted relative to shares available to trade. A wave of buying — coordinated partly through online forums — triggered exactly this feedback loop, and the share price rose by an extraordinary multiple in a matter of days, before falling sharply again afterward.

What makes this a useful case study isn't the specific company — it's what it reveals about market mechanics and psychology: prices can move dramatically for reasons that have little to do with a company's underlying business, when positioning (in this case, heavy short interest) and sentiment (collective buying enthusiasm) collide. Understanding this helps make sense of *why* markets sometimes move in ways that seem to defy "the fundamentals" — at least in the short term.

## The takeaway

Shorting is a real and legitimate tool used by sophisticated investors for hedging, betting against overvaluation, or constructing market-neutral strategies — but it comes with asymmetric, theoretically unlimited risk that makes it fundamentally different from buying. Short squeezes are a vivid reminder that markets are made of people and positions, not just spreadsheets — and that "the price" and "the value" of something can disagree, sometimes dramatically, before eventually converging again.
