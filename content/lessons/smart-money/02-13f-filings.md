# What a 13F filing tells you

> **Before we start:** this lesson explains a type of public regulatory filing and the academic research around it, as a case study in market psychology and information. It is not investment advice, and "what a famous investor owns" is never a reason on its own to buy or sell anything.

## What a 13F actually is

In the United States, any institutional investment manager overseeing more than $100 million in certain securities must file a **Form 13F** with the SEC every quarter, listing their holdings — which stocks, how many shares, and their approximate value — as of the end of that quarter.

This requirement exists for transparency: regulators (and the public) get a window into what large investors are doing with their money. Financial media often turn 13F season into headlines — "Famous Investor X buys [company]," "Famous Investor Y exits [company]."

## The 45-day lag — the single most important fact about 13Fs

A 13F covers a *quarter* (three months), and isn't due until **45 days after that quarter ends**. So when a 13F is published, it's describing a snapshot of what a fund owned *up to 45 days ago* — and the fund could have changed its position completely the day after that snapshot was taken, without anyone knowing for months.

Think about what that means: by the time the public sees "Investor X bought Company Y in Q1," it might already be mid-Q2. Investor X could have already sold. The information isn't *wrong* — it's just **old**, and the gap between "true" and "old" is exactly where a lot of misunderstanding happens.

## What a 13F shows — and what it can never show

A 13F shows **long positions in certain US-listed equities and similar instruments**, as of one date, each quarter. That's it. Here's what it does *not* show:

- **Short positions** — bets that a stock will fall. A fund could be massively short something while a 13F shows no hint of it.
- **Derivatives, in most cases** — options and other instruments are often reported in ways that obscure the actual economic bet being made.
- **Cash levels** — a fund sitting 80% in cash, waiting for opportunities, looks identical in a 13F to one that's fully invested, if you only look at what it *does* hold.
- **Non-US holdings, in many cases** — international positions often fall outside 13F reporting entirely.
- **Intraday or rapid trading** — a fund that bought and sold the same stock five times during the quarter just shows up as whatever it held at the final snapshot.

In short: a 13F is a single, low-resolution, time-delayed photograph of part of a portfolio. Treating it as a live signal is like reading a 45-day-old newspaper and reacting as if it's breaking news.

## So why does "cloning" come up at all?

Here's where it gets genuinely interesting. There's real academic research looking at what happens if you mechanically copy the *changes* in holdings of certain types of investors — specifically, ones with **low turnover** (they don't trade often) and **concentrated** portfolios (a small number of large positions, suggesting high conviction). Some studies have found that portfolios constructed this way — buying what such investors bought, after the 13F becomes public, and holding for a similar long period — have historically shown excess returns compared to a simple market benchmark, *on average, over long periods*.

Two things are doing a lot of work in that sentence: "low turnover, concentrated" and "on average, over long periods." This research is about a *style* — patient, high-conviction, long-term investors — not about copying *any* 13F, and not about any single trade.

## Higher-signal cousins: 13Ds and insider cluster-buys

Two related types of filings are generally considered more timely and information-rich than ordinary 13Fs:

- **Form 13D** is filed when an investor acquires more than 5% of a company *and* intends to influence or control it (an "activist" stake) — filed within days, not 45 days later, and signals active intent, not just passive holding.
- **Insider cluster buying** — when *multiple* executives or directors of a company buy shares with their own money around the same time — is often considered more informative than a single insider's trade, since it's harder to explain away as personal financial planning when several people make the same call independently.

Both are examples of filings that are faster and carry more "intent" information than a routine 13F — which is part of why treating all filings as equally meaningful is a mistake.

## The takeaway

13F season makes for great headlines because "what the smart money bought" is an irresistible story. The reality is more like: a quarterly, partial, 45-day-old photo of part of a portfolio, most useful when looking at *patterns over time* from *patient, concentrated* investors — and even then, as one input into your own thinking, never a signal to act on by itself.
