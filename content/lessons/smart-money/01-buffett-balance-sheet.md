# How Warren Buffett reads a balance sheet

> **Before we start:** this lesson is about *how to read* a financial statement — a skill, like reading a map. It is not a recommendation to buy, sell, or hold any investment. The goal is that the news, and conversations about companies, make more sense afterward — not that you act on anything here.

## What a balance sheet actually is

Every company's balance sheet follows one equation, and everything else is detail:

**Assets = Liabilities + Equity**

In plain terms: everything a company *owns* (assets) was paid for either with money it *owes* (liabilities — loans, unpaid bills) or money that belongs to its *owners* (equity — shareholders' stake). It's a snapshot, at one moment in time, of what a company has and who has a claim on it.

Compare this to an **income statement**, which shows activity *over a period* (revenue, costs, profit for the year). The balance sheet is a photo; the income statement is a video.

## The lens: businesses as things you'd want to own forever

A famous long-term investing approach — most associated with Warren Buffett — starts from a simple question: *if you couldn't sell this for ten years, would you still want to own it?* That question changes what you look for in a balance sheet, away from "will the price go up next month" and toward "is this a fundamentally sound business."

A few things this lens pays attention to:

### 1. Debt levels (relative to equity)

A company funded mostly by shareholder money (equity) has more breathing room than one funded mostly by borrowed money (debt) — debt has to be repaid on schedule regardless of how business is going, while equity doesn't. A useful ratio here is **debt-to-equity**: total liabilities divided by total equity. There's no single "good" number — it varies hugely by industry (a bank's balance sheet looks completely different from a software company's) — but a company whose debt has been climbing steadily relative to its equity, without a clear reason, is worth a second look.

### 2. Return on equity (ROE)

ROE = net profit ÷ shareholders' equity. It answers: "for every shilling of owners' money invested in this business, how much profit did it generate?" A consistently high ROE — especially one that doesn't rely on heavy borrowing to inflate it — is often a sign of what's sometimes called a "moat": some durable advantage (a brand, a network effect, a cost advantage) that lets a company earn more on its capital than competitors can.

### 3. Cash vs. reported profit

A company can report a profit on paper while actually running low on cash — for example, if a large portion of "sales" are owed by customers who haven't paid yet (accounts receivable), or if profit includes one-off items that won't recur. Comparing reported net income to actual cash flow from operations is a quick sanity check: are these two numbers telling roughly the same story, or pulling apart?

## A worked illustration

Imagine a fictional company, "Jaza Foods Ltd," with the following (simplified, illustrative) balance sheet:

- Total assets: KES 500 million
- Total liabilities: KES 150 million
- Shareholders' equity: KES 350 million

Debt-to-equity = 150 ÷ 350 ≈ **0.43**. For context, this means the company has roughly 43 cents of liabilities for every shilling of shareholder equity — a relatively conservative position, though "conservative" still depends on the industry.

If Jaza Foods reported net profit of KES 52.5 million for the year, ROE = 52.5 ÷ 350 = **15%**. Whether 15% is "good" depends on what similar companies in the same industry typically achieve — which is why comparison matters more than the raw number in isolation.

## What this lens deliberately ignores — and why that's also useful

This approach tends to downplay short-term price movements, news cycles, and predictions about next quarter. That's not because those things don't matter at all — it's because they're far harder to have a genuine edge in, especially for an individual investor without specialized tools or information. A balance sheet, by contrast, is public, available to everyone at the same time, and doesn't change minute to minute. Learning to read it is a skill that compounds — the more balance sheets you read, the faster you spot what's normal and what's unusual.

## Why this is education, not a strategy to copy

Notice that nothing above said "therefore buy companies with ROE above X%." Real analysis involves dozens of factors, industry context, management quality, competitive dynamics, and more — and even professional investors who use this exact lens disagree with each other constantly about specific companies. The value of this lesson isn't a checklist for picking stocks. It's that the next time you read that a company's "debt levels are concerning" or its "returns on capital are strong," you'll know roughly what that means and why someone might say it — which is the actual goal: understanding how the conversation works, not winning it.
