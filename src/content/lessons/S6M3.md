# Money Beliefs & Where They Come From

Long before you earned a shilling, you were taught what money *means* — by watching your parents argue or stay silent about it, by what your culture praised or shamed, by whether money felt scarce or safe. These lessons became **money scripts**: deep, often unconscious beliefs that quietly run your financial life today. Two people with the same salary make wildly different choices because they're running different scripts. This lesson helps you see yours — and rewrite the ones holding you back.

## What money scripts are

A money script is a belief about money you absorbed early and now treat as simply "true" — even though you never chose it. They usually run *under* your awareness, which is what makes them powerful. You don't think "I believe money is dangerous"; you just feel anxious every time you have some and rush to spend it.

Common scripts sound like:
- *"There's never enough."* → hoarding, fear, never feeling secure even when you are.
- *"Money is the root of evil / rich people are bad."* → self-sabotage, guilt about earning or keeping money.
- *"Money is meant to be enjoyed now."* → spending everything, no saving.
- *"Talking about money is rude/taboo."* → silence that breeds conflict and ignorance.
- *"More money will fix everything."* → chasing income while never managing what you have.

None of these are facts. They're inherited lenses — and you can change the lens.

## Where they come from

Your scripts were written by:
- **Family.** How your parents handled, discussed (or hid) money. A child who watched money cause stress often inherits money-anxiety; one who saw reckless spending may copy or rebel against it.
- **Scarcity or plenty.** Growing up with too little can wire either over-spending ("enjoy it before it's gone") or extreme fear of spending. Both are scarcity's fingerprint.
- **Culture and community.** What's celebrated (generosity, display, success) and what's shamed shapes what you chase and avoid.
- **Big early experiences.** A family financial shock, a windfall, a loss — these stamp lasting beliefs.

## The Kenyan scripts worth naming

Some beliefs are especially common in the Kenyan context, and naming them takes away their hidden power:
- **"Showing you've made it" matters.** Strong cultural value on *visible* success — the right car, clothes, events — which can push spending past what's wise (the status trap from Module 1).
- **Money is for sharing / "we don't keep, we give."** Generosity and family support are deeply valued — beautiful, but unbounded it becomes black tax that breaks budgets.
- **Wealth as luck or "blessing."** Framing money as luck (a theme betting exploits) rather than as something you build through habits.
- **Debt as shame, or debt as normal.** Some inherit deep fear of any debt; others, that constant borrowing is just how life works. Neither extreme serves you.
- **Money is private, even from your spouse.** Silence that, as Series 9 shows, quietly damages relationships.

You may recognise several. That recognition is the work.

## How beliefs drive behaviour

Your scripts decide your defaults before logic gets a vote:
- A "never enough" script makes you anxious and either hoard or panic-spend.
- A "money is for now" script makes saving feel like deprivation.
- A "talking about money is rude" script keeps you ignorant and silent in exactly the conversations (salary, family support, joint finances) that most affect you.

When you keep making the same money mistake despite "knowing better," a script is usually running the show.

## Rewriting an unhelpful script

You can't delete a belief, but you can *replace* it with a more useful one through awareness and practice:
1. **Catch it.** Notice the feeling and the automatic thought next time money stirs emotion. ("I just felt I *had* to spend this.")
2. **Trace it.** Ask where it came from. ("Money never lasted at home, so holding it feels pointless.")
3. **Question it.** Is it actually true *for you, now*? Usually not.
4. **Rewrite it.** Choose a truer, kinder belief. ("Money I keep is safety and freedom I'm building," instead of "money is meant to disappear.")
5. **Act it into truth.** Beliefs change by behaving differently — automate a small saving, hold money without spending it, and prove the new script real.

This isn't a one-time fix; it's a practice. But becoming aware of your scripts is the single biggest step — you can't change a rule you can't see.

## Key takeaways

- You run inherited, mostly unconscious **money scripts** that drive your decisions.
- They come from **family, scarcity or plenty, culture, and big early experiences.**
- Name the **Kenyan scripts** — status/display, unbounded sharing, money-as-luck, debt extremes, money-secrecy.
- When you repeat a money mistake "despite knowing better," a **script is running** the show.
- Rewrite scripts by **catching, tracing, questioning, replacing, and acting** the new belief into truth.
