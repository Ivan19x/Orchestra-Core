# Furnishing & Equipping a Home Affordably

A new home is empty, and the pressure to fill it *now* — with the nice sofa, the big TV, the full set of everything — is intense. That pressure pushes many people into expensive hire-purchase debt or a drained account, starting their new home financially stressed. But a home doesn't have to be furnished all at once, or with debt, to be comfortable. This lesson shows how to equip a home affordably, in the right order, without the debt trap that catches so many.

## The hire-purchase trap

Furniture and appliance shops love **hire-purchase** — "take it home today, pay small-small every month." It feels affordable because you focus on the monthly payment, not the total. But hire-purchase is usually **expensive debt in disguise**: by the time you finish paying, you've often paid far more than the cash price — sometimes 1.5× or more — for an item that's *losing* value the whole time (a textbook bad debt, Series 1, Module 5).

The maths to always do: **what's the cash price vs the total of all the monthly payments?** That gap is the real cost of "convenience." Usually it's enough to make saving up and buying cash the obvious choice.

## Save up, then buy — the cheaper path

The alternative is simple but requires a little patience: **save the monthly amount you *would* have paid hire-purchase, then buy the item cash** once you've saved enough. You get the same item, often for far less, and you own it outright with no debt hanging over you. While you save, the money can even earn a little in an MMF. A short wait saves real money — and the habit of "save then buy" instead of "buy then pay" is one that serves you for life.

## Buy in the right order: essentials first

You don't need a fully furnished home on day one. Separate **essentials** from **nice-to-haves** and buy in order of genuine need:

**Buy first (you can't live without):**
- A bed and bedding
- A way to cook (cooker/gas + a few pots and utensils)
- Somewhere to sit
- Basic lighting and storage
- A few essential kitchen items

**Buy later (as money allows):**
- A sofa set, a big TV, decor
- Extra furniture, appliances, the "complete" look

Furnishing gradually, paying cash as you go, means you're never in debt and never overwhelmed. The "complete home" can take months to assemble — and that's completely fine.

## Where to find affordable options

- **Second-hand / used.** Quality used furniture and appliances cost a fraction of new, and for many items (a solid table, a sofa frame) condition matters more than newness. Markets, online marketplaces, and moving sales are gold.
- **Local artisans / jua kali.** Custom furniture from local makers is often cheaper and more durable than mass-market shop furniture.
- **Buy quality on the things that last.** For items you'll use daily for years (a bed, a cooker), decent quality bought cash is cheaper over time than cheap-and-replace (cost-per-use, Series 1, Module 4).
- **Inherit and accept hand-me-downs.** No shame in starting with family's spare items; upgrade later from savings.

## A sensible setup plan

1. **List essentials** and rough cash prices.
2. **Buy the true must-haves** (bed, cooking, basic seating) within your move-in budget.
3. **Save monthly** for the next items, buying cash as each fund fills.
4. **Avoid hire-purchase** unless you've done the maths and it genuinely beats saving (it rarely does).
5. **Mix second-hand and new** — quality used for big items, new for what must be.

## Key takeaways

- The pressure to furnish a home *all at once* pushes people into **expensive hire-purchase debt** — avoid it.
- **Hire-purchase often costs far more than cash** for a depreciating item — always compare cash price vs total payments.
- **Save up, then buy cash** — same item, less money, no debt; the money can even earn while you save.
- **Buy essentials first** (bed, cooking, seating); the "complete home" can come gradually.
- Use **second-hand, local artisans, and hand-me-downs**, and buy quality on the things you'll use daily.
