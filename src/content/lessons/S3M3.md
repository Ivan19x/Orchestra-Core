# Understanding Assets That Pay You

In Module 1 you learned the wealthy buy **assets** — things that put money *into* your pocket. This lesson gets specific: which income-producing assets an ordinary Kenyan can actually own, what they realistically pay, and how to start small. The dream is simple — own enough assets that their income covers your life, so work becomes a choice. It begins with your very first SACCO share or fund deposit.

## The four ways an asset pays you

Income-producing assets pay you through one of four channels:
1. **Interest** — you lend money and get paid for it (money market funds, bank deposits, government bonds).
2. **Dividends** — you own a slice of a company or co-op and share its profits (SACCO shares, NSE-listed shares).
3. **Rent** — you own property and let others use it (a room, a unit, land, equipment).
4. **Business profit** — you own a venture that earns more than it costs to run.

You don't need all four. The goal is to start owning *any* of them, then add more over time.

## What they realistically pay in Kenya (2026)

Honest, current numbers matter — ignore anyone promising more without more risk:

| Asset | Typical return (2026) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Money market fund** | ~9% gross (~7.6% after 15% tax) | Low risk, access in 1–3 days |
| **SACCO deposits (rebate)** | ~10–13% | Locked-in; also unlocks cheap loans |
| **SACCO share capital (dividend)** | often **15–20%+** at top SACCOs | e.g. Tower ~20%, Police ~17%, Stima ~16% (FY2025) |
| **Government bonds (T-bonds)** | competitive; infrastructure bonds tax-free | Very low risk; via DhowCSD |
| **NSE shares (dividends + growth)** | varies widely; can fall | Higher risk, longer horizon |
| **Rental property** | rental yield varies by area | High capital + management hassle |

Two things jump out. First, a **SACCO** can pay genuinely strong returns — both a deposit rebate *and* a dividend on share capital — which is why it's such a popular wealth tool here. Second, the **safe, accessible options (MMF, SACCO, bonds)** already beat inflation comfortably, so you don't need risky bets to grow money.

## Start small — assets are not just for the rich

You can begin building income assets with very little:
- **MMF:** some accept minimums from around **KES 100–1,000**, paying interest from day one.
- **SACCO:** modest monthly contributions build share capital (dividends) and deposits (rebates + loan access).
- **Bonds:** **DhowCSD** lets ordinary Kenyans buy government securities directly, with lower entry than before.
- **Shares:** a **CDS account** and a broker let you buy NSE shares in small amounts.

The first KES 1,000 into a fund or SACCO is the real milestone — not because of the income it earns yet, but because you've crossed from *consumer* to *owner*. Then you feed it monthly.

## Buy assets before luxuries

Here's the mindset that separates the wealthy from the flashy: **buy the asset first, let the asset buy the luxury.** Instead of financing a nicer phone now, put that money into income assets; later, the income they throw off can fund the nice things — and you still own the assets. The wealthy don't avoid enjoyment; they make their assets pay for it, rather than their salary or a loan.

A simple habit: every time you're tempted by a depreciating want, ask, "could this money buy an asset instead?" You won't always say yes — but asking, regularly, quietly redirects money from things that drain you to things that pay you.

## Key takeaways

- Assets pay you four ways: **interest, dividends, rent, business profit.**
- In Kenya, **safe options already pay well** — MMF ~9%, SACCO deposits ~10–13%, top SACCO dividends 15–20%+ — no risky bets needed.
- You can **start small** (MMF from ~KES 100; modest SACCO contributions; DhowCSD for bonds).
- The real milestone is **becoming an owner**, then feeding the asset monthly.
- **Buy assets before luxuries** — let the asset's income fund the nice things.
