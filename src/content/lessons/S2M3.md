# Rent & Moving Out: The Real Costs

Moving into your own place is a milestone — and a financial trap if you do it on excitement instead of arithmetic. The advertised rent is only a slice of what moving out actually costs, and renting more than you can afford is one of the fastest ways to stay broke on a decent salary. This lesson lays out the real numbers — what to budget, what it costs to move in, and how much rent your income can actually carry.

## Realistic rents in Nairobi (2026)

Rent varies enormously by area, but as a rough guide:
- **Bedsitter:** from about **KES 5,500–12,000** in cheaper estates (e.g. Jogoo Road, parts of Eastlands), **KES 12,000–20,000** in mid areas, and **KES 20,000+** in upmarket zones (Kilimani, Kileleshwa, Westlands).
- **One-bedroom:** roughly **KES 14,000–25,000** mid-market, rising to **KES 30,000–50,000+** in pricier areas.
- **Two-bedroom:** roughly **KES 25,000–80,000** depending on area.

The same size can cost three times more across town. **Where** you rent is the biggest lever on your housing cost — and proximity to work also changes your transport bill, so weigh both together.

## The upfront cost is the real shock

People budget for monthly rent and forget the **move-in bill**, which is far larger. To move in you typically need:
- **Deposit** — usually **one to two months' rent**, held as security.
- **First month's rent** — paid up front.
- **Moving costs** — transport, a *mover*, maybe an agent's fee.
- **Furnishing & setup** — bed, cooker, gas, utensils, seats, curtains.

> Example: a KES 20,000 bedsitter can need `40,000 (deposit) + 20,000 (first rent) + ~10,000 (moving) + furnishing` — easily **KES 80,000–100,000+** before your first night. Underestimating this is why so many move in already stressed and borrowing.

The fix (from Series 1): a **sinking fund** — save toward the full move-in figure *before* you commit, so you arrive with a buffer, not a hole.

## The rent-to-income rule

How much rent can you actually afford? A widely used guideline: **rent should be no more than about a third (≈30%) of your take-home pay.** On a KES 45,000 net salary, that's roughly **KES 13,500–15,000** of rent. Go much higher and you become "house poor" — a nice address with nothing left for food, savings, or emergencies.

It's a guideline, not a law: in expensive cities people stretch it, but every extra shilling of rent is a shilling not saved or invested. Renting *below* your limit and banking the difference is one of the simplest wealth moves available to a young earner.

## Tenant basics worth knowing

- **Get a written tenancy agreement.** Read it: rent, deposit terms, notice period, who pays for repairs and water/electricity.
- **Document the condition** when you move in (photos), so your deposit isn't unfairly withheld later.
- **Know your deposit rights.** It's *security*, meant to be refunded (less genuine damage) when you leave properly — not the landlord's bonus.
- **Notice periods cut both ways** — usually you must give notice (often a month) before leaving, and the landlord before eviction.
- **Beware rental scams** — never pay a "viewing fee" or deposit for a house you haven't seen, to someone you can't verify.

## A smarter sequence

- **Run the full numbers first:** monthly rent *plus* the move-in total, against your income and savings.
- **Don't move out to escape — move out to plan.** If sharing a little longer lets you build an emergency fund and move in calmly, that's the stronger play (see Series 7, Module 2).
- **Keep rent under your ceiling** and automate the gap into savings.

## Key takeaways

- Nairobi rents swing hugely by area — **bedsitters ~KES 5,500–20,000+**, one-beds ~KES 14,000–50,000 — so location is your biggest cost lever.
- The **move-in bill** (deposit + first rent + moving + furnishing) is often **several months' rent** — sink-fund it first.
- Keep **rent ≤ ~30% of take-home**; renting below your ceiling and banking the rest builds wealth.
- Get a **written agreement**, document condition, and know your **deposit rights**.
- Move out on a **plan**, not an impulse.
