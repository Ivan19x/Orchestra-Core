# Wills, Inheritance & Succession

You spend a lifetime building — savings, a plot, a SACCO, maybe a home — to provide for your family. Yet without a little planning, that very legacy can become the thing that tears your family apart after you're gone: drawn-out court battles, relatives fighting, children left unprotected, and assets frozen for years. A **will** and some simple succession planning are how you ensure what you built actually reaches the people you intended, smoothly. This lesson explains how inheritance works in Kenya and the steps to protect your family.

> This is general education, **not legal advice.** For an actual will, use a qualified advocate — it's worth the modest cost.

## Why a will matters

A **will** is a legal document stating how you want your property (your "estate") distributed after you die, and who should manage it. Its purpose is simple but vital: to make **your wishes clear and legally binding**, so your family doesn't have to guess, fight, or fall under default rules that may not match what you wanted.

In Kenya, succession is governed by the **Law of Succession Act**. The single most important fact: **dying *with* a valid will (testate) is far smoother for your family than dying *without* one (intestate).**

## What happens without a will (intestate)

If you die without a valid will, the **law decides** how your estate is shared — not you. Broadly, under the Act:
- A **surviving spouse and children** are the primary beneficiaries — the spouse typically receives personal effects and an interest in the estate, with the children sharing the remainder.
- If there's **no spouse or children**, the estate passes to **parents**, then **siblings**, and so on down a defined line.

The problems with leaving it to the default:
- **It may not reflect your wishes** (e.g. a dependent you wanted to provide for, or a specific gift).
- **It invites disputes** — relatives contest shares, and family conflict over inheritance is extremely common.
- **It's slower and costlier** — courts must determine and confirm everything from scratch.

A will replaces all that uncertainty with *your* clear instructions.

## What makes a will valid in Kenya

For a **written will** to be valid under the Law of Succession Act, generally:
- The maker (**testator**) must be **18+ and of sound mind**.
- It must be **in writing** and **signed** by the testator.
- It must be **witnessed by at least two people**, who sign in the testator's presence — and importantly, **witnesses (and their spouses) should not be beneficiaries**, or their gift can fail.

(Oral wills exist but are limited and risky — they must be made before witnesses and are only valid in narrow circumstances. A **written will is far safer.**)

A good will also **names an executor** — the trusted person who will carry out your wishes — and clearly lists your assets and who gets what, including a guardian for minor children.

## The probate process (how an estate is settled)

After death, the estate must go through **succession** to legally transfer assets:
- **With a will:** the named executor applies to the court for a **grant of probate** — authority to administer the estate per the will.
- **Without a will:** a relative applies for **letters of administration** to administer the estate under the law's default rules.
- The grant is typically **confirmed after about six months**, after which assets can be distributed.

This process takes time and some cost — but a clear will, an organised list of assets, and named beneficiaries (below) make it far faster and less contentious.

## The smart shortcut: nominate beneficiaries

Here's a powerful, underused tool. Several assets let you **nominate a beneficiary directly**, so that money passes to your chosen person *quickly*, often **outside the lengthy succession/probate process**:
- **SACCO deposits and shares** — nominate next of kin/beneficiaries.
- **Pension and NSSF** — nominate beneficiaries.
- **Life insurance** — the payout goes to your named beneficiaries (Series 9, Module 6).
- **Bank accounts** — next-of-kin nominations where available.

By keeping these nominations **filled in and up to date**, you ensure that a meaningful chunk of what you leave reaches your family **fast and dispute-free**, rather than being frozen for months. Combine nominations (for these specific assets) with a will (for everything else) for the smoothest possible outcome.

## Practical steps to protect your family

- **Write a will** — ideally with an advocate; even a simple valid one beats none. Update it as life changes (marriage, children, new assets).
- **Name an executor** you trust, and a **guardian** for minor children.
- **Keep an organised list** of your assets, accounts, and where documents are — so your family can actually find everything.
- **Fill in beneficiary nominations** on your SACCO, pension, insurance, and bank — and review them.
- **Talk to your family** about your wishes where appropriate — clarity now prevents conflict later.

## Key takeaways

- A **will** makes your wishes legally binding; **dying without one (intestate)** hands the decision to the law and invites disputes and delay.
- A valid written will (Kenya): **18+, sound mind, in writing, signed, witnessed by 2+ non-beneficiaries**, naming an executor.
- Estates pass through **probate** (grant of probate with a will; letters of administration without) — confirmed after ~6 months.
- **Nominate beneficiaries** on SACCO, pension, insurance and bank accounts so that money reaches family **fast, outside probate.**
- Protect your family: **write and update a will, name an executor/guardian, list your assets, and keep nominations current.**
