# The Nairobi Securities Exchange

Owning a piece of a real company — Safaricom, a big bank, a manufacturer — used to feel like something only the wealthy did. It isn't. Through the **Nairobi Securities Exchange (NSE)**, any Kenyan with an ID, a KRA PIN, and a few thousand shillings can become a part-owner of listed companies and share in their profits. But shares are also where beginners lose money fastest if they treat them like betting. This lesson explains how the NSE really works — and how to approach it with eyes open.

## What the NSE is

The **NSE** is Kenya's stock market: a regulated marketplace where shares (and bonds) of listed companies are bought and sold. When you buy a share, you own a tiny slice of that company. It's regulated by the **CMA**, and your shares are held electronically by the **CDSC** (Central Depository) — not as paper certificates.

Two main indices tell you how the market is doing overall:
- **NSE 20** — tracks 20 top companies.
- **NASI (All-Share Index)** — tracks all listed companies.

When the news says "the NSE gained," it usually means these indices rose.

## How shares make you money

Two ways:
1. **Dividends** — your share of the company's profits, paid out (often once or twice a year). A profitable, stable company pays regular dividends — passive income for simply owning the share.
2. **Capital growth** — if the share price rises above what you paid, your holding is worth more. (It can also *fall* below — that's the risk.)

Strong long-term returns usually come from *both*: dividends collected over years plus gradual price growth — not from quick trading.

## How to actually buy shares (step by step)

1. **Choose a licensed stockbroker.** Reputable names include Faida Investment Bank, Genghis Capital, SBG Securities, Dyer & Blair, and AIB-AXYS Africa. Confirm they're **CMA-licensed**.
2. **Open a CDS account** through the broker — usually **free**, needing your ID and KRA PIN. This electronic account holds your shares.
3. **Fund your account** with the broker.
4. **Place an order** for the shares you want (the minimum trade is **100 shares**).
5. **Settlement** happens through the CDSC, and the shares appear in your account. Many brokers now offer **online/app trading** so you can do it from your phone.

## What it costs

Buying and selling shares isn't free — **brokerage commission plus market levies** total roughly **2%–2.5% of the transaction value**. That matters: trading in and out frequently bleeds money in fees, which is another reason buy-and-hold beats constant trading. Factor the cost in, especially on small trades.

## The real risks (read this before you buy)

Shares can grow your wealth, but they carry genuine risk that savings products don't:
- **Prices fall as well as rise.** A single company can disappoint, get hit by scandal, or just drift down for years. You can lose money — even on a "blue chip."
- **Single-stock risk is real.** Putting everything into one share (because it's popular or "everyone's buying it") is dangerous. **Diversify** — spread across several companies/sectors, or use a collective fund.
- **It's a long game.** Shares are for money you can leave for **years**, riding out the ups and downs. Money you need next year does **not** belong in shares.
- **Hype is the enemy.** "Hot tips" and FOMO into a rising stock are how beginners buy high and panic-sell low. Decide on fundamentals and a long horizon, not on noise (see Series 3, Module 4).

## A sensible beginner approach

- **Only invest money you won't need for years** — after your emergency fund and bad debt are sorted (Series 8, Module 1).
- **Start small and diversified** — a few solid, dividend-paying companies, or a collective vehicle, rather than one "tip."
- **Reinvest dividends** to compound.
- **Think like an owner, not a trader** — you're buying a slice of a business for the long run, not gambling on this week's price.
- If picking individual shares feels overwhelming, **collective options** (unit trusts, index-style funds) give you a basket in one purchase.

## Key takeaways

- The **NSE** lets any Kenyan own shares; the **CMA** regulates it and the **CDSC** holds your shares electronically.
- Shares pay via **dividends** (profit share) and **capital growth** — best captured over years.
- To buy: pick a **CMA-licensed broker**, open a **free CDS account** (ID + KRA PIN), fund it, order (min **100 shares**).
- Costs are about **2–2.5% per trade** — so **buy and hold**, don't churn.
- Shares carry **real risk**: diversify, invest only long-term money, and **ignore hype**.
