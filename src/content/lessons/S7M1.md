# Running a Household Budget

Budgeting for yourself is hard enough. Budgeting for a *home* — with rent, food for several people, school fees, a spouse or housemates, and family who call when things go wrong — is a different sport. The money is bigger, the costs are shared, and one person's slip-up affects everyone. But the payoff is also bigger: a household that runs its money on a plan is calmer, argues less, and gets ahead.

This lesson builds a household budget that works in the real Kenyan home — irregular incomes, lumpy bills, and all.

## Why a household budget is different

Three things change when money is shared:
1. **Multiple incomes, multiple wants.** Two earners with different habits must agree on one plan.
2. **Bigger, lumpier costs.** Rent, school fees, and family events dwarf an individual's spending and don't arrive evenly.
3. **Shared consequences.** If the food budget is blown, *everyone* feels it. That makes honesty and teamwork essential.

So a household budget is as much about **communication** as arithmetic. The numbers are easy; agreeing on them is the work.

## Step 1 — Add up the household's real income

Total every shilling that reliably comes in each month: both salaries (use **net**, take-home pay — see Series 2, Module 1), plus any steady side income. If income is **irregular** (business, hustles, commissions), budget on a *cautious* figure — roughly your typical low month — so a slow month doesn't break the plan. Treat anything above that as a bonus to save.

## Step 2 — Map where it goes (the Kenyan household's big rocks)

In most Kenyan homes, spending clusters into a few large categories. Nationally, **food and housing are the two biggest** household costs (food carries the largest weight in the official cost-of-living basket), so they deserve the most attention:

- **Housing** — rent (or mortgage), and the deposit you'll one day need to move.
- **Food** — the single biggest controllable cost; *unga*, staples, vegetables, the monthly shop.
- **Utilities** — electricity tokens, water, cooking gas, internet (see Module 4).
- **Transport** — fare or fuel for everyone.
- **School fees** — large and *lumpy*: it lands in big chunks each term, not monthly.
- **Health** — SHIF plus out-of-pocket costs.
- **Family / black tax** — support to extended family.
- **Debt repayments** — loans, SACCO, any Fuliza.
- **Savings & emergency fund** — pay the household's future first.

## Step 3 — Plan for the lumpy costs *before* they hit

The thing that wrecks household budgets is not the monthly stuff — it is the **big, occasional** costs: school fees, a wedding contribution, *Christmas*, a medical bill. The fix is the **sinking fund**: divide a known future cost by the months until it's due and save that slice every month.

> Example: school fees of about KES 30,000 a term, three terms a year = KES 90,000 a year. Save `90,000 ÷ 12 ≈` **KES 7,500 a month** into a separate pot, and fees stop being a crisis — they're just a transfer.

Do the same for events and the festive season. A household that sinks-funds its lumpy costs almost never needs a panic loan.

## Step 4 — Decide together and write it down

Sit down as a household and agree:
- **Who pays what.** Common approaches: split bills proportionally to income, or assign categories ("you cover rent and power; I cover food and school fees"). There is no single right way — only the way you both agree to and stick to.
- **Joint vs separate.** Many couples keep a **joint pot** for shared bills (each contributing a set amount) plus **separate** personal money for guilt-free individual spending. This reduces friction.
- **A monthly money check-in.** Fifteen minutes to compare plan vs reality. This habit prevents most household money fights.

## A simple worked household budget

A two-earner home with **KES 90,000** combined take-home might land near:

| Category | Amount (KES) |
|---|---|
| Rent | 22,000 |
| Food | 22,000 |
| Transport | 8,000 |
| Utilities (power, water, gas, internet) | 7,000 |
| School-fees sinking fund | 7,500 |
| Family support / black tax | 5,000 |
| Health (SHIF + out-of-pocket) | 3,500 |
| Savings & emergency fund | 9,000 |
| Personal / guilt-free (split) | 6,000 |
| **Total** | **90,000** |

These are *illustrative* — your real numbers depend on your town, rent, and family size. The point is the shape: housing and food lead, lumpy costs are pre-funded, and savings is a line item, not an afterthought.

## Key takeaways

- A household budget is about **communication** as much as arithmetic — agree, write it down, review monthly.
- Budget on **net, cautious** income, especially if earnings are irregular.
- **Food and housing** are the biggest costs — control those first.
- **Sinking funds** turn lumpy costs (school fees, events) from crises into transfers.
- Decide **who pays what** and consider a **joint pot plus separate personal money** to reduce friction.
