# Domestic Help & Household Services Costs

As a household grows — children, a busier life — many Kenyan families employ domestic help: a house help, a nanny, a cook, a gardener, or a watchman. It's a normal part of running a home here. But few employers realise that hiring someone makes you an **employer with legal obligations** — not just a monthly salary, but a minimum wage, statutory contributions, and leave entitlements. Get it wrong and you risk penalties; get it right and you treat someone fairly while budgeting properly. This final lesson of *Home & Household* covers the cost and the rules.

## You're not just paying a salary — you're an employer

The key shift in thinking: when you hire domestic help, **you become an employer**, and Kenyan labour law applies. That means there's a **legal minimum wage**, **statutory deductions** you must handle, and **rights** (leave, rest, notice) your worker is entitled to. Treating domestic work as "informal" and ignoring these is both unfair and, increasingly, legally risky — there are real penalties for underpaying.

## The minimum wage (2026)

The government **gazettes a minimum wage** for domestic workers, which rises over time. As of 2026 (after a ~12% increase):
- **Major cities** (Nairobi, Mombasa, Kisumu, Eldoret): about **KES 18,047 per month.**
- **Other large urban centres** (e.g. Ruiru, Mavoko, Limuru): about **KES 16,650.**
- **Smaller towns and rural areas:** about **KES 9,268.**

These are *minimums* — you can pay more, and skills (a trained nanny, a cook) often command more. Paying below the gazetted minimum is unlawful. Note these usually refer to the cash wage; where you provide housing/food, the rules treat that specifically — but you can't simply pay far below the minimum in cash and call the difference "food."

## Your legal obligations as an employer

Beyond the wage, employing someone brings duties many households overlook:
- **NSSF** — you must register your worker and remit **NSSF contributions** (the 6% scheme, with your employer share), so they build retirement savings (Series 2, Module 1).
- **SHIF** — health contributions (2.75%) apply, giving your worker health cover.
- **Leave and rest** — domestic workers are entitled to **annual leave, rest days, and reasonable hours**, like other employees.
- **A fair agreement** — ideally a simple written contract covering pay, duties, hours, leave, and notice.
- **Notice/terminal dues** — proper notice (or pay in lieu) and any dues on ending employment.

Handling NSSF and SHIF for your house help isn't just legal compliance — it gives a low-income worker retirement savings and health cover they'd otherwise lack. It's the right thing *and* the lawful thing.

## Budgeting for domestic help honestly

When you budget for domestic help, count the **full cost**, not just the headline wage:
- **The wage** (at least the gazetted minimum for your area).
- **Statutory contributions** (NSSF, SHIF — partly your cost as employer).
- **Housing and food** if you provide them (a real cost to budget).
- **Leave cover** — someone to cover during their leave, perhaps.
- **The occasional bonus / fair treatment** that keeps good help.

A live-in house help in Nairobi, all-in, can therefore cost meaningfully more than the KES 18,000 wage once food, housing, and contributions are counted. Budget the true figure so it's sustainable.

## Other household services

Beyond regular employees, households pay for **occasional services** — a cleaner, a *fundi* for repairs, a plumber, laundry, security. Tips:
- **Get quotes and compare** — service prices vary widely; agree the price *before* the work.
- **Decide regular vs occasional** — a full-time employee vs. occasional help changes both cost and obligations (occasional contractors aren't your employees in the same way).
- **Pay fairly** — fair pay gets reliable, honest service and avoids the cost of constant turnover.
- **Factor these into the household budget** as their own line.

## Doing it right

- **Pay at least the gazetted minimum** for your area — it's the law and it's fair.
- **Register for and remit NSSF and SHIF** for regular employees.
- **Respect leave, rest, and reasonable hours**, ideally with a simple written agreement.
- **Budget the full cost** (wage + contributions + housing/food) so it's sustainable.
- **Treat people well** — fair, lawful employment gets you reliable help and peace of mind.

## Key takeaways

- Hiring domestic help makes you an **employer** under Kenyan labour law — with a minimum wage, statutory contributions, and leave duties.
- **Minimum wage (2026):** ~**KES 18,047** in major cities, ~KES 16,650 in other urban areas, ~KES 9,268 in smaller towns/rural — these are minimums.
- You must handle **NSSF and SHIF** for regular employees, plus **leave, rest, and fair notice** — ideally a written agreement.
- **Budget the full cost** (wage + contributions + housing/food), which exceeds the headline wage.
- For **occasional services**, agree prices upfront, pay fairly, and budget them separately.
