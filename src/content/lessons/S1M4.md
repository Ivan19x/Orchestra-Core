# Needs vs Wants & Smart Spending

Every budget, every saving plan, every financial goal eventually runs into one question at the shop counter: *do I actually need this?* Most money problems aren't caused by big disasters — they're caused by a thousand small spending decisions made on autopilot. Learning to tell a real need from a want, and to spend *consciously* on what genuinely matters to you, is the everyday skill that makes everything else in money possible. This lesson sharpens it.

## Needs vs wants: the honest line

- A **need** is something you genuinely must have to live and function: basic food, shelter, transport to work, essential clothing, healthcare.
- A **want** is everything else — the upgrades, the nice-to-haves, the things that make life more pleasant but aren't essential.

The trap is that wants love to disguise themselves as needs. "I *need* a new phone" — but does the old one still work? "I *need* to eat out" — or do you want the convenience? A simple test: **could you survive and function without it, or with a cheaper version?** If yes, it's a want (or a want dressed as a need). This isn't about denying yourself wants — it's about *seeing them clearly* so you choose them on purpose.

## What Kenyans actually spend on

It helps to know where money really goes. In Kenya's official cost-of-living basket (KNBS), **food is the single biggest category — around 32%** of the typical household's spending — followed by **transport** and **housing/utilities**; together these three make up well over half of all spending. So the biggest *needs* for most people are food, transport and housing — which is exactly where conscious spending saves the most (see the food and transport lessons). The lesson: focus your spending discipline on the big categories, not just the small treats.

## Conscious spending: spend on what you value, cut the rest

Smart spending isn't about cutting *everything* — that's misery, and it fails. It's about being **ruthless on what you don't care about so you can be generous on what you do.** If coffee with friends genuinely brings you joy, keep it — and cut hard somewhere you *don't* value (subscriptions you forgot, impulse snacks, fees). This is "conscious spending": every shilling either goes to a real need or a want you've genuinely chosen, and nothing leaks to wants you don't even notice.

The enemy isn't spending — it's *unconscious* spending: money that drains away on autopilot, on things that don't actually make your life better.

## Beating impulse buys

Impulse spending is a want acting fast, before your sense can catch up. The defences:
- **The 24-hour rule.** For any non-essential, wait a day. The urge fades; genuine wants survive. For bigger buys, wait a week.
- **Don't shop to feel better.** Notice when you spend out of stress, boredom or FOMO (the psychology series) — the feeling, not the item, is what you're really chasing.
- **Remove temptation.** Log out of shopping apps, turn off one-click checkout, unfollow accounts that make you spend.
- **Carry a list and a number.** Decide what you'll buy *before* you enter the shop, and stick to it.

## Getting real value: think cost-per-use

Smart spenders judge price differently — not by the sticker, but by **value over time**:
- **Cost-per-use.** Cheap shoes at KES 1,500 that last 3 months cost more *per wear* than KES 4,000 shoes that last 3 years. Buying quality on the things you use constantly is often cheaper long-term — but only on things you *actually* use a lot.
- **Opportunity cost.** Every shilling spent here is a shilling not saved, invested, or spent on something better. "What else could this KES 5,000 do?" is a powerful question.
- **Beware the 'cheap' trap and the 'expensive equals better' trap.** Cheap-but-useless and pricey-for-the-badge both waste money. Value is the goal, not price.

## Key takeaways

- A **need** is essential; a **want** is everything else — and wants often disguise themselves as needs.
- Most spending goes to **food (~32%), transport and housing** — focus discipline on the big categories.
- Practise **conscious spending:** cut hard on what you don't value to spend freely on what you do.
- Beat impulse with the **24-hour rule**, removing temptation, and shopping a list.
- Judge purchases by **cost-per-use and opportunity cost**, not the sticker price.
