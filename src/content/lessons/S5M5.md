# HELB & Financing Your Education

For many young Kenyans, education is the first big "loan" of their lives — and HELB is how they fund it. Used well, a HELB loan is genuinely *good debt*: cheap money that buys an education which raises your income for life. Misunderstood, it becomes a nasty surprise — penalties, CRB listing, and a deduction from your first salary you didn't plan for. This lesson explains exactly how HELB works, what it costs, and how to combine it with the free money (bursaries) most students leave on the table.

## What HELB is

The **Higher Education Loans Board (HELB)** is the government body that lends to Kenyan students for higher education and provides some bursaries. It's how millions afford university and college. Its loans are **subsidised** — far cheaper than any commercial loan — which is what makes them good debt *if* you respect the repayment terms.

## What HELB offers (2026)

- **Undergraduate loan:** up to about **KES 60,000 per year** for first-time/regular applicants — part goes directly to tuition, part to you as upkeep.
- **TVET (college) loan:** up to about **KES 40,000** (e.g. ~KES 26,420 to tuition, ~KES 13,580 upkeep).
- **Jielimishe and other products:** can fund a larger share of fees (up to ~90%, capped around KES 600,000 for some products).
- Under the newer university funding model, HELB loans combine with **government scholarships** and family contribution, scaled to a student's assessed need (Series 9, Module 3).

You **apply online** through HELB's portal each year — and crucially, you must **reapply every year** you need it.

## The cost: cheap, but not free

This is where HELB beats every commercial loan:
- **Interest: ~4% per year** — a fraction of a bank loan (~15%) and almost nothing next to a mobile loan (~395%).
- Plus a small **annual ledger fee** (~KES 1,000).

At 4%, HELB is some of the cheapest credit you'll ever access. Borrowing it to fund an income-raising education is a textbook example of **good debt**.

## Repayment and the grace period

- **Grace period:** repayment generally starts **one year after you finish** studies — giving you time to find work first.
- **How you repay:** if employed, it's **deducted from your salary** (your employer remits it). Minimums run about **KES 4,000–5,000/month** for the salaried, or around **KES 1,500/month** if unemployed.
- **Term:** typically **4 to 10 years** (48–120 months), depending on the balance and your ability.

Plan for this: your first payslip will likely carry a HELB deduction. It's not a surprise tax — it's repaying the loan that funded your degree. Budget for it from your first job.

## The real cost of default — take this seriously

HELB is cheap *only if you repay properly*. Default and it gets expensive fast:
- A **penalty of ~KES 5,000 per month** for every month the loan is overdue once repayment is due — these stack up brutally.
- **CRB listing** — defaulting can get you blacklisted, blocking other loans (a SACCO, a mortgage) and sometimes affecting job/clearance checks, for years.

The good news: HELB periodically offers **penalty waivers** (e.g. an 80% waiver for clearing the balance in one payment) — worth watching for if you've fallen behind. But the simplest path is to start repaying on time. Defaulting on a 4% loan and racking up KES 5,000/month penalties turns the cheapest debt into an expensive mess.

## Don't forget the free money: bursaries & scholarships

Loans aren't the only option — and unlike loans, **bursaries and scholarships don't need repaying.** Many students miss out simply by not applying:
- **Constituency bursaries (NG-CDF)** and **county bursaries** — apply through your area; these reduce fees directly.
- **Scholarships** — government, corporate, and NGO scholarships for strong or needy students.
- **University financial aid / work-study** where available.

The smart approach: **chase every bursary and scholarship you qualify for first** (free money), then use HELB to bridge the gap, and family contribution for the rest. Stacking these minimises how much you borrow.

## Key takeaways

- **HELB is good debt** — subsidised loans (~**4%/yr**) for an income-raising education.
- Amounts: undergrad up to ~**KES 60,000/year**, TVET ~KES 40,000; **reapply every year**.
- Repayment starts **~1 year after finishing**, often via salary deduction (~KES 4,000–5,000/month) — **budget for it.**
- **Default is costly:** ~**KES 5,000/month penalties** + CRB listing — repay on time (watch for penalty waivers).
- **Chase bursaries and scholarships first** (free money), then use HELB to bridge the gap.
