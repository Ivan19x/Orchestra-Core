# The Emergency Fund

It's the least exciting money topic and the most important. An **emergency fund** — a stash of cash set aside for life's inevitable shocks — is the single foundation that makes every other financial decision safe. Without one, a job loss, a hospital bill, or a broken-down *boda* sends you straight to a mobile loan at 395% or into a debt spiral. With one, the same shock is just an inconvenience. This lesson shows why the emergency fund comes *first*, how much you need, and how to build it even from nothing.

## What it is and why it's first

An emergency fund is money saved specifically for **unexpected, essential expenses** — not a holiday, not a phone, but a genuine emergency: losing your income, a medical crisis, an urgent home or vehicle repair, a family emergency.

It's the **first savings goal**, before investing, because it's your **shock absorber**. Research on how Kenyans cope with emergencies (FSD Kenya's FinAccess surveys) shows most people, lacking savings, turn to **borrowing or family** when a shock hits — which is exactly how the debt traps catch them. An emergency fund breaks that pattern: it's the buffer that means a bad week doesn't become a bad year.

It also protects your *other* money: with a buffer, you never have to sell investments at a bad time or raid your SACCO to handle a surprise. It's the seatbelt that lets you drive everything else safely.

## How much do you need?

The classic guideline is **3 to 6 months of essential expenses** — enough to keep the lights on, rent paid, and family fed if your income stopped. "Essential expenses" means your *needs* (rent, food, transport, utilities, debt minimums), not your full lifestyle.

Adapt it to your reality:
- **Stable salaried job, few dependants?** 3 months may be enough.
- **Irregular income (hustle, business), or dependants relying on you?** Aim for the **higher end (6 months or more)** — your income is less predictable and more people depend on it.
- **Just starting?** Don't be paralysed by the big number. A **first milestone of KES 10,000–30,000** already removes the most common small-shock panic. Build to one month, then three, then six.

Work out your monthly essentials, multiply by your target months — that's your goal.

## Where to keep it

The emergency fund has two needs that pull in opposite directions: it must be **accessible** (you need it fast in a crisis) but **not too accessible** (so you don't spend it on non-emergencies), and it should **beat inflation** while it waits.

The best Kenyan homes for it:

| Where | Access | Earns? | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Money market fund** | 1–3 days | ~9% (beats inflation) | **Ideal** — accessible *and* growing |
| **M-Shwari / mobile** | Instant | Low | Good for the first, small buffer |
| Bank savings account | Instant | Low/0% | Okay but loses to inflation |
| **SACCO deposits** | Locked-in | High | **Not** ideal — too locked for emergencies |
| Cash / wallet | Instant | 0% | Too tempting; loses value |

For most people, a **money market fund is the perfect emergency fund home**: your money is there within a day or two when you truly need it, but the small delay stops impulse raids — and meanwhile it earns ~9%, beating inflation (Series 8, Module 3). Keep it **separate** from your everyday spending money so it's mentally "untouchable."

## Building it from nothing

If you have nothing saved, the fund can feel impossible. It isn't — it's built the same way as any savings, a slice at a time (Series 1, Module 3):
- **Pay yourself first** — automate a fixed amount to the fund every payday, before you can spend it.
- **Start tiny.** Even KES 500 a week is KES 26,000 a year. Consistency beats size.
- **Use windfalls.** Save a big chunk of any bonus, gift, or good hustle month straight into the fund.
- **Bank found money.** Redirect a cancelled subscription, a cut bill, or money you'd have bet — straight into the fund.
- **Set a clear milestone** (your first KES 20,000) so progress feels real and motivating.
- **Only touch it for real emergencies** — and if you do, rebuild it first before resuming other goals.

## Key takeaways

- An emergency fund is your **shock absorber** — the first savings goal, before investing, because it stops shocks becoming debt spirals.
- Target **3–6 months of essential expenses** — lean toward 6+ with irregular income or dependants; start with a small first milestone.
- Keep it **accessible but separate, and inflation-beating** — a **money market fund is ideal**.
- **Build from nothing** by paying yourself first, starting tiny, and banking windfalls and found money.
- Only spend it on **genuine emergencies** — then rebuild it.
