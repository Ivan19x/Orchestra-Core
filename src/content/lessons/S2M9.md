# Transport & Car Ownership Costs

Owning a car is, for many Kenyans, the ultimate symbol of having arrived. But few decisions quietly drain a budget like buying a car before you can truly afford it. The price on the windscreen is only the beginning — fuel, insurance, service, and depreciation make the *real* cost of ownership far higher than people expect. This lesson lays out the honest numbers, compares owning to using public transport, explains car financing, and confronts the uncomfortable truth: for most people, a personal car is a **liability**, not an asset.

## The real cost of a car (it's not just the price)

When people budget for a car, they think of the purchase price and fuel. The full cost is much bigger:

- **Fuel** — the obvious one, and it's high. In mid-2026, Nairobi petrol was about **KES 214/litre** and diesel about **KES 233/litre** (EPRA reviews these monthly). A modest car doing ~1,500 km a month can burn **KES 25,000+** in fuel alone.
- **Insurance** — third-party is compulsory; comprehensive (sensible for a valuable car) runs roughly **4–7% of the car's value per year.**
- **Service & maintenance** — oil, tyres, brakes, repairs, the unexpected breakdown. Budget several thousand shillings a month on average.
- **Depreciation** — the silent giant. A car **loses value every year** (often ~10–15%), whether you drive it or not. On an KES 800,000 car, that's ~KES 100,000 of value gone in year one — a real cost, even though no cash leaves your pocket monthly.
- **Other** — parking, NTSA fees, license, the occasional fine.

> Add it up: a basic car can easily cost **KES 35,000–50,000+ a month** all-in — *before* any loan repayment. Many owners only feel the fuel and are quietly bleeding on the rest.

## Matatu vs owning: run the comparison

For most people, public transport (matatu, *boda*, bus) costs a fraction of car ownership:
- **Daily fare** might total **KES 5,000–12,000 a month** for commuting — versus the KES 35,000–50,000+ all-in cost of a car.
- That **gap** — often KES 25,000+ a month — invested instead (an MMF at ~9%, Series 8) builds serious wealth over years.

This doesn't mean never own a car — it means be honest about the trade-off. A car buys convenience, comfort, time, and sometimes income (if it earns, e.g. for a business or hustle). But buying one purely for status, before your finances are solid, is one of the most expensive lifestyle decisions you can make.

## How car financing works (and its trap)

Many buy on **asset finance / a car loan** from a bank or SACCO:
- You pay a **deposit** (often 10–30%), then monthly installments with interest over a few years.
- A **SACCO** loan (~1%/month reducing) is usually far cheaper than a bank or dealer financing arrangement (Series 1, Module 5).
- **The trap:** financing a *depreciating* asset means you're paying interest on something losing value — bad debt by definition. You can end up owing more than the car is worth, while also carrying all the running costs.

If you finance a car, do it with the cheapest credit, a realistic budget for the *full* monthly cost (loan + running costs), and ideally for a car that earns or is genuinely needed — not for image.

## Why a car is usually a liability

Recall the asset/liability test (Series 3, Module 1): an **asset puts money in your pocket; a liability takes money out.** A personal car:
- **Takes money out** every month (fuel, insurance, service).
- **Loses value** every year (depreciation).
- Earns nothing (unless you use it to generate income).

So a personal-use car is a textbook **liability**, however nice it feels. The same car used productively — for a delivery business, a taxi, ferrying goods for a hustle that earns more than the car costs — *can* be an asset. The difference is whether money flows in or out.

## A sensible approach

- **Get your foundations first** — emergency fund, no bad debt, savings habit — *before* taking on a car.
- **Budget the full cost**, not just fuel and the price — the all-in monthly number is what matters.
- **Consider whether you truly need it** vs. using cheaper transport and investing the difference.
- **If you buy:** buy within your means (a reliable used car beats a financed flashy one), use the cheapest financing, and keep total transport cost a sensible share of income.
- **Make it earn** if you can — a car that generates income changes the maths entirely.

## Key takeaways

- A car's **real cost** is fuel + insurance + service + **depreciation** — easily **KES 35,000–50,000+ a month** before any loan.
- **Public transport** often costs a fraction; the gap, invested, builds real wealth.
- **Financing a car** means paying interest on a depreciating asset — bad debt; use the cheapest credit and budget the *full* cost.
- A **personal-use car is a liability** (money out, value down) — unless it *earns* income, which makes it an asset.
- Get your **foundations first**, budget honestly, and consider whether you need a car or just want one.
