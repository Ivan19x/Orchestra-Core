# Teaching Kids About Money

Most of us learned money the hard way — by making expensive mistakes as adults, because nobody taught us. You can give your children something better: the financial sense to start adult life ahead instead of behind. And here's the freeing part — you don't need to be wealthy or a finance expert to raise money-smart kids. You need to teach a few simple ideas, at the right ages, and (most powerfully) to *show* them good habits. This lesson is how.

## Why it matters — and why it's mostly caught, not taught

Money habits form early, long before any classroom covers them. Children absorb their attitudes to money mainly by **watching you** — how you spend, save, stress or stay calm, talk or stay silent about money. That means the most important "lesson" isn't a lecture; it's the example you live. A child who sees a parent budget calmly, save consistently, and resist impulse buys learns more than any speech could teach. Conversely, a child who only sees spending learns to spend.

So step one is simply: **model the habits you want them to have.** They're always watching.

## Age-appropriate money lessons

Teach the right idea at the right stage:

**Young children (≈4–7): money is earned and finite.**
- Money comes from work, not magic; it runs out when spent.
- A clear jar they can *see* fill teaches saving better than any account.
- Simple choices: "you can buy this small thing now, or save for that bigger thing."

**Older children (≈8–12): saving, waiting and choosing.**
- A **save / spend / give** split for any money they get — the foundation habit (Series 5, Module 1).
- Saving toward a real goal, and feeling the reward of buying it themselves.
- Basic wants vs needs; the idea that things cost effort.

**Teenagers (≈13–18): real-world money.**
- A small **budget** of their own to manage (and to make cheap mistakes with while stakes are low).
- Earning — a holiday hustle, helping in a business — to feel money's true cost.
- The dangers ahead: betting, mobile loans, lifestyle pressure (Series 5).
- The basics of accounts, M-Pesa, and how saving grows over time.

## Pocket money that actually teaches

Pocket money (allowance) is a powerful teaching tool *if* it's structured, not just handed over:
- **Give it regularly** so they learn to plan across time, not just spend on receipt.
- **Let them control it** — and let them run out. The lesson of "I spent it all and now can't buy the thing I wanted" is priceless, and far cheaper learned at 10 than at 30.
- **Encourage the save/spend/give split** rather than dictating every shilling.
- **Consider linking some to effort/chores** so they connect money with work (balance this with your family's values).
- **Resist rescuing them** every time they overspend — the natural consequence *is* the teaching.

## Practical tools

- **A savings jar/box** for young kids — visible, tangible saving.
- **A children's/youth savings account** as they grow, so they experience real saving and watch a balance build (many banks and SACCOs offer these).
- **A simple goal chart** — drawing the thing they're saving for, and tracking progress, makes delayed gratification concrete and fun.
- **Involve them in family money** age-appropriately — letting them see budgeting, comparing prices at the shop, planning a saving goal as a family.

## The gift you're really giving

Teaching kids about money isn't about raising little misers — it's about raising adults who feel *calm and capable* with money, who can save, wait, choose, and avoid the traps that catch so many. That confidence is one of the most valuable inheritances you can leave — worth more than money itself, because it's the skill that *makes* money. And teaching it costs nothing but attention.

## Key takeaways

- Money habits are **mostly caught, not taught** — your example is the main lesson, so model good habits.
- Teach **age-appropriately:** money is earned/finite (young), save-wait-choose (older), real budgeting and the traps (teens).
- Use **pocket money** as a teaching tool — give it regularly, let them control it, and **let them run out** to learn.
- Practical tools: a **savings jar**, a **youth account**, a **goal chart**, and involving them in family money.
- The real gift is a child who is **calm and capable with money** — an inheritance worth more than cash.
