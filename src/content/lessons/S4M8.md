# Chamas, Table Banking & Group Investing

The **chama** is one of Kenya's great financial inventions — a way for ordinary people to pool money, save together, and invest in things none of them could afford alone. Chamas collectively hold a vast amount of Kenyans' wealth and have funded countless plots, businesses, and educations. But they're also where friendships and savings die when they're run badly — through poor records, theft, or conflict. This final lesson of *Kenya Money* explains how chamas work, why they're so effective, and how to run one that lasts.

## What a chama is

A **chama** is an informal (or formally registered) group — friends, colleagues, family, neighbours — who **pool money regularly** toward a shared financial goal. Members contribute a set amount on a schedule, and the pooled money is saved, lent out, or invested for the group's benefit. Chamas are deeply woven into Kenyan life and are especially powerful for people the formal system underserves.

Their power comes from one thing money struggles to provide alone: **social accountability.** You're far more likely to keep saving when your friends are watching and depending on you — peer pressure turned into a force for good.

## The three main models

### 1. Merry-go-round (rotating savings)
The simplest form: everyone contributes the same amount each period, and the **whole pot goes to one member**, rotating until everyone has had a turn. No one earns interest, but each person gets a useful lump sum when it's their turn — forced saving with a guaranteed payout. Great for discipline and lump-sum needs; it doesn't *grow* money.

### 2. Investment chama
The group **pools contributions and invests them** — in land, shares, a money market fund, a business, rental property, or government securities. The wealth grows over time and is shared by ownership stake. This is the model that **builds real wealth**: many Kenyan plots and businesses were bought by investment chamas that no single member could have afforded.

### 3. Table banking
Members contribute to a common fund, and that fund is **lent out to members** (at a small interest) right there "on the table," then repaid with interest that grows the pool. It combines saving with access to cheap credit for members — a mini, informal SACCO.

Many chamas blend these — e.g. a merry-go-round plus a growing investment pot.

## Why chamas work (and where their magic lies)

- **Social discipline** — the group keeps everyone contributing; you save because others are counting on you.
- **Collective power** — pooled money buys assets (land, property) individuals couldn't reach alone.
- **Accessibility** — no minimum salary, no bank gatekeeping; anyone can join or start one.
- **Knowledge sharing** — members learn from each other's financial experience.
- **Trust** — built among people who already know each other.

That last strength is also the biggest weakness: chamas run on trust, and **broken trust is what kills them.**

## How chamas fail — and how to avoid it

Most chama disasters come from a few avoidable mistakes:
- **Poor or no records.** Money pooled without clear bookkeeping breeds suspicion and loss. **Fix:** keep meticulous records — who paid what, what's in the pot, every transaction — visible to all.
- **Theft / misuse of funds.** When one person (often the treasurer) controls the money alone, temptation and risk are high. **Fix:** use a **group bank/SACCO account requiring multiple signatories**, never one person's personal account.
- **No rules or constitution.** Vague arrangements cause disputes. **Fix:** write a **constitution** — contribution amounts, schedules, what happens if someone defaults or leaves, how decisions are made, how money is handled.
- **Weak governance.** **Fix:** elect clear roles (chairperson, treasurer, secretary), hold regular minuted meetings, and rotate or check the treasurer.
- **Mismatched goals.** Members wanting different things pull the group apart. **Fix:** agree the chama's purpose upfront.

## How to set one up well

1. **Agree the purpose and model** (merry-go-round, investment, table banking, or a blend).
2. **Write a constitution** with rules, contributions, and consequences.
3. **Open a group account** (bank or SACCO) with **multiple signatories** — never an individual's account.
4. **Keep transparent records** everyone can see.
5. **Consider registering** the group (e.g. as a self-help group or investment group) for legal standing, the ability to own assets formally, and to open accounts — bodies like **KAIG (Kenya Association of Investment Groups)** support chamas.
6. **Start small, build trust**, and grow the ambitions as the group proves reliable.

## Key takeaways

- A **chama** pools members' money to save and invest together — powered by **social accountability.**
- Three models: **merry-go-round** (rotating lump sums), **investment chama** (pooled investing — builds real wealth), and **table banking** (lending to members).
- Chamas work through **discipline, collective power, accessibility, and trust** — but **broken trust kills them.**
- Avoid failure with **clear records, a multi-signatory group account, a written constitution, and good governance.**
- Set one up by agreeing purpose, writing rules, opening a proper account, keeping records, and considering **formal registration.**
