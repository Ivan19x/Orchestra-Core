# Good Debt vs Bad Debt

"Debt" is treated as a single dirty word, but that's a costly oversimplification. Some debt builds wealth; some destroys it — and confusing the two leads people to fear the helpful kind and embrace the harmful kind. A mortgage that buys an appreciating home and a Fuliza overdraft that buys a night out are both "debt," but they're financial opposites. Learning to tell good debt from bad — and to borrow only wisely — is one of the most important money skills you'll ever build. This lesson draws the line.

## The simple test

Ask one question of any debt: **does it build your wealth, or drain it?**

- **Good debt** helps you acquire something that **grows in value or earns income** — and usually carries a **lower** interest rate.
- **Bad debt** funds **consumption or things that lose value** — and usually carries a **high** interest rate.

| Good debt (builds) | Bad debt (drains) |
|---|---|
| A **mortgage** for a home (KMRC-backed ~9%) | A **mobile loan** for daily spending (Fuliza ~395% annualised) |
| A **SACCO loan** (~1%/month) for a business or plot | A **financed phone** you don't need |
| A **HELB loan** (~4%) for education that raises your income | **Betting** "loans," or borrowing for a holiday |
| Asset finance for equipment that earns | Borrowing to keep up appearances |

Good debt is a tool that makes you richer over time. Bad debt is a leak that makes you poorer — and the high rate means it compounds against you fast (Series 8, Module 2, in reverse).

## How interest really works (APR)

To judge any debt, you must understand its **real** cost, expressed as an annual rate (APR — Annual Percentage Rate). This is where people get fooled. A "small" daily or monthly fee hides a huge annual rate:
- A **SACCO loan** at ~1% per month on a *reducing balance* is genuinely cheap — a true cost in the low teens per year.
- A **bank loan** averages around **14.78%** a year (2026) — moderate.
- A **mobile loan / Fuliza** with a tiny daily fee annualises to roughly **395%** — astronomically expensive.

**Always ask "what does this cost per year?"** before borrowing. The same KES 10,000, borrowed from a SACCO vs a loan app, can differ in cost by a factor of *twenty*. The headline "fee" is designed to look harmless; the APR is the truth.

## How to borrow wisely

If you borrow, do it on purpose, not in panic:
- **Borrow for assets, not consumption.** A loan that buys something earning or appreciating can make sense; a loan for a depreciating want rarely does.
- **Get the cheapest source.** For serious borrowing, a **SACCO loan (~1%/month)** or a structured bank loan beats mobile loans every time. The Hustler Fund (~8%/yr) beats apps for small sums.
- **Know the total cost**, not just the monthly payment. Fees + interest over the whole term is the real price.
- **Keep repayments affordable** — a common guide is total debt repayments under ~30–35% of your income, so debt doesn't strangle your budget.
- **Have an emergency fund** so you don't borrow for every small shock (the #1 reason people fall into bad debt).

## The CRB: your borrowing reputation

When you borrow from regulated lenders, your behaviour is recorded by **Credit Reference Bureaus (CRBs)** — TransUnion, Metropol, Creditinfo. Repay well and you build a good record that unlocks future credit; default and you can be **listed**, which blocks loans (and sometimes jobs) for years. Good debt, repaid responsibly, actually *builds* your reputation. (Full detail in Series 2, Module 8.)

## Key takeaways

- **Not all debt is bad** — the test is whether it **builds wealth or drains it.**
- **Good debt** (mortgage ~9%, SACCO ~1%/mo, HELB 4%) buys assets/income at low rates; **bad debt** (mobile loans ~395%, financed wants) funds consumption at high rates.
- Judge every loan by its **annual cost (APR)** — small fees hide huge rates.
- **Borrow wisely:** for assets, from the cheapest source, knowing the total cost, with affordable repayments and an emergency fund.
- Repaying well **builds your CRB reputation**; defaulting blocks credit for years.
