# Insurance Basics

Insurance is one of the most misunderstood parts of money. Some people see it as a waste ("I pay and get nothing back"); others are over-sold policies they don't need. Both are expensive mistakes. Insurance, used well, is simply how you stop a single bad event — a serious illness, an accident, a death — from wiping out everything you've built. It trades a small, predictable cost for protection against a large, unpredictable disaster. This lesson explains how it works and which cover actually matters.

## Why insurance exists: trading small certain losses for protection

The core idea is **risk transfer**. Alone, you couldn't survive a KES 2 million hospital bill or replacing a written-off car. So many people each pay a small amount (a **premium**) into a pool run by an insurer; when disaster strikes one of them, the pool pays. You're trading a small *certain* cost (the premium) to avoid a large *uncertain* one (the catastrophe).

The key insight: insurance is **not** an investment, and "getting nothing back" means nothing bad happened — that's a *win*, not a loss. You insure against things that would be financially catastrophic, not against small costs you could absorb yourself.

In Kenya, insurers are regulated by the **IRA (Insurance Regulatory Authority)** — only deal with IRA-licensed insurers.

## How premiums work

Your **premium** is the regular amount you pay for cover. It's priced on **risk**: the more likely or costly the event, the higher the premium. A few terms worth knowing:
- **Sum insured / cover limit** — the maximum the insurer will pay.
- **Excess / deductible** — the portion you pay yourself on a claim (a higher excess usually means a lower premium).
- **Exclusions** — what the policy does *not* cover (always read these).
- **Premium frequency** — monthly/annually.

Cheaper isn't automatically better — a low premium with huge exclusions or a tiny cover limit may be worthless when you actually claim.

## The cover that actually matters

You don't need every product an agent offers. Prioritise the few that protect against genuine catastrophe:

### 1. Health insurance (the most important for most people)
A serious illness is the most common financial disaster Kenyans face. Two layers:
- **SHIF (Social Health Authority)** — the mandatory national scheme, **2.75% of income**, which provides a base of cover (Series 2, Module 1).
- **Private health cover** — tops up SHIF for broader/faster treatment, private hospitals, and higher limits. Worth it if you can afford it, especially with dependants.

Without health cover, one hospital admission can erase years of savings — this is the cover to get first.

### 2. Motor insurance
If you own a vehicle, **third-party motor insurance is compulsory by law** — it covers injury/damage you cause to others. Two levels:
- **Third-party only** — the legal minimum; covers others, not your own car.
- **Comprehensive** — covers your own vehicle too (theft, accident damage). More expensive, but sensible for a valuable car (Series 2, Module 9).

### 3. Life insurance
If people **depend on your income**, life cover pays out to them if you die, so they're not left in crisis. The cheapest, most efficient form is **term life** — pure cover for a set period at a low premium. (Covered in depth for families in Series 9, Module 6.) If no one depends on you financially yet, you may not need it.

## Avoiding under- and over-insurance

The two mistakes:
- **Under-insured** — no health cover, or cover limits too low to actually help. One disaster and you're exposed.
- **Over-insured** — paying for cover you don't need (expensive whole-life policies sold as "investments," cover for things you could easily self-fund, duplicate policies). Money wasted on unnecessary premiums is money not saved or invested.

The balance: **insure the catastrophes (health, your income if dependants, a valuable asset); self-insure the small stuff** (build an emergency fund for the costs you could absorb). And read every policy's limits and exclusions before buying — a policy you don't understand is a policy that may fail you when you claim.

## Key takeaways

- Insurance is **risk transfer** — a small certain cost to avoid a large uncertain disaster; it's **not** an investment.
- **Premiums** are priced on risk; mind the cover limit, excess and exclusions — cheap with huge exclusions is worthless.
- Priorities: **health cover first** (SHIF + private top-up), **motor** (third-party is compulsory), and **life** if people depend on you.
- **Insure catastrophes, self-insure small stuff** (via your emergency fund).
- Avoid being **under- or over-insured**, and use only **IRA-licensed** insurers.
