# Managing Utility Bills

In a household, utilities are a stubborn monthly cost — power, water, gas and internet land every month, and with more people in the home they add up fast. The good news: utility bills are one of the most *controllable* household costs. A few habit changes and smart choices can knock a real chunk off them every month — money that goes straight to your savings — without anyone living in the dark or going hungry. This lesson is about cutting household utility consumption sensibly.

> Series 2, Module 4 explains how each bill is *charged* and set up. This lesson focuses on *reducing a household's usage.*

## Electricity: the biggest savings live here

Power is usually the utility with the most waste, because consumption is invisible until the tokens run out. Remember Kenya Power bills in **tiers** — the more you use, the higher the rate per unit (and KES 1,000 of tokens buys only ~35–45 units all-in) — so cutting use saves you *twice*: fewer units, and possibly a cheaper band.

Where a home's power actually goes — target the big eaters:
- **Heating water** (instant showers, water heaters) — often the single biggest cost. Heat only what you need; consider solar where possible.
- **The iron** — a heavy short-burst user. Iron in batches, not daily.
- **The fridge** — runs 24/7; keep it well-sealed, not overpacked, and away from heat.
- **Lighting** — switch fully to **LED/energy-saving bulbs**; switch off empty rooms.
- **Phantom load** — devices left on standby (TV, decoder, chargers) sip power constantly. Switch off at the wall.

Simple household rules — "lights off when you leave," "iron once a week," "short hot showers" — cut a power bill noticeably with zero hardship.

## Water: stop the silent leaks

Water waste is usually invisible until the bill arrives:
- **Fix leaks fast.** A dripping tap or a running toilet cistern can quietly double a bill — check periodically.
- **Simple habits:** turn off the tap while brushing/soaping, fix the toilet float, reuse water where sensible (e.g. for cleaning or plants).
- **Harvest rainwater** where you can for non-drinking uses.
- Know whether water is **metered or flat** in your home — metered means your habits directly cut the bill.

## Cooking gas: make a cylinder last longer

Gas is a refill cost (6kg ~KES 1,100–1,390; 13kg ~KES 2,500–3,200), so the goal is **fewer refills**:
- **Cook efficiently:** lids on pots, right-sized flames, and a **pressure cooker** for beans/*githeri* dramatically cuts gas time.
- **Buy the right cylinder size** — the **13kg is cheaper per kilo** than the 6kg if the household cooks a lot.
- **Batch-cook** staples to use one heat-up for several meals.
- Shop around on refill price; it varies by brand and dealer.

## Internet: right-size the package

Fibre is often over-bought — homes pay for speed they never use:
- **Match the plan to real need.** A mid plan (and there are options from ~KES 800–3,000/month) suits most households; the top-tier gigabit packages are rarely necessary.
- **One shared connection** beats everyone burning mobile data — for a household, fibre is usually far cheaper per GB.
- **Review your plan** periodically; providers add cheaper/faster options often, and you may be on an outdated, pricier package.

## Make it a household habit

Utilities are a *shared* cost, so saving on them is a *team* effort:
- **Agree simple rules** everyone follows (lights, ironing, showers, taps).
- **Make usage visible** — note how fast tokens deplete; awareness alone cuts use.
- **Track utilities as one budget line** and aim to trim it a little each season.
- Remember every shilling saved on a bill is a shilling that can go to the **emergency fund or savings** — utilities are a quiet, repeatable source of savings.

## Key takeaways

- Utility bills are **highly controllable** — and tiered electricity means cutting use saves you twice.
- **Power's big eaters:** water heating, ironing, the fridge, lighting and standby load — target those.
- **Water:** fix leaks fast (they silently double bills) and build simple habits.
- **Gas:** lids, pressure cookers, the right cylinder size, and shopping around mean fewer refills.
- **Internet:** right-size the plan and share one connection; review it regularly.
- Make saving on utilities a **shared household habit** and redirect the savings.
