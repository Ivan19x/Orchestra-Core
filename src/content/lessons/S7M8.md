# Planning for Household Emergencies

A financial shock that one person could absorb can devastate a whole household — because more people depend on the income, and the costs are bigger. A job loss, a serious illness, a death, a major repair: any of these can plunge an unprepared family into debt, disruption, or worse. The families that weather storms aren't lucky — they *prepared*. This lesson is about building household resilience: the fund, the insurance, and the plan that turn a potential disaster into a manageable setback.

> Series 1, Module 7 covers the personal emergency fund. This lesson scales it to the whole **household** and adds the other layers of protection.

## Why households need a bigger plan

With dependants, the stakes rise:
- **More people rely on the income** — if it stops, more people are affected.
- **Costs are bigger and harder to cut** — rent, food for several, school fees keep coming.
- **Shocks are more varied** — not just *your* job or health, but a partner's, a child's illness, a family emergency upcountry.

So a household needs **layers of protection**, not just one buffer: a bigger emergency fund, the right insurance, and a plan for the big shocks.

## Layer 1: the household emergency fund

The first line of defence. Size it to the **household's** essential monthly costs:
- Aim for **3–6 months of essential household expenses** — and lean toward the **higher end (6+ months)** because more people depend on it and family costs are sticky (school fees don't pause).
- Keep it **accessible but separate**, in an **inflation-beating MMF** (Series 1, Module 7), so it's there within days when needed.
- **Build it together** as a household goal — and protect it for genuine emergencies only.

This fund is what stops a rough patch from becoming a debt spiral or a child leaving school.

## Layer 2: insurance — transferring the big risks

An emergency fund can't cover a catastrophic KES 2 million hospital bill or the loss of the breadwinner's income forever. That's what **insurance** is for (Series 2, Module 6) — transferring risks too big to self-fund:
- **Health cover** — the most important. **SHIF** (the national scheme) plus, ideally, a **private top-up**, because a serious illness is the most common household financial disaster. With children, medical needs are frequent.
- **Life insurance** on the breadwinner(s) — so the family isn't left without income if someone dies (Series 9, Module 6). Term life is cheap and essential when people depend on you.
- **Home/contents and motor** as relevant — protecting valuable assets you couldn't easily replace.
- **Last-expense cover** — modest cover for funeral costs, which can otherwise hit a grieving family hard (Series 9, Module 8).

The principle: **insure the catastrophes; self-insure the small stuff** (via the emergency fund).

## Layer 3: preparing for specific shocks

Beyond fund and insurance, think through the big "what ifs":

**Job loss / income loss.** The most common household shock. Preparation:
- The emergency fund (above) is your runway while you find new income.
- Keep your **skills and network** current so you can earn again quickly (Series 3, Module 6).
- Having **multiple income streams** (Series 3, Module 2) means losing one isn't total.
- Know which costs you'd cut fast if income stopped — a mental "emergency budget."

**Medical crisis.** Health cover (SHIF + private) plus the emergency fund for what cover doesn't reach.

**Death of a breadwinner.** Life insurance, named beneficiaries, and a will (Series 9, Modules 6 & 7) so the family is provided for and assets aren't frozen.

**Major repair / loss.** The emergency fund, plus relevant insurance for big assets.

## Make it a household habit

- **Build the fund together** and keep it topped up.
- **Review your cover** as the family grows (more children, a mortgage change everything).
- **Talk through the "what ifs"** as a household so everyone knows the plan — not a morbid exercise, a reassuring one.
- **Keep documents organised** (policies, accounts, beneficiaries) so they can be found in a crisis.

A prepared household faces shocks with a plan instead of panic — and that security is one of the greatest gifts you can give your family.

## Key takeaways

- Households need **layers of protection** because more people depend on the income and costs are bigger and stickier.
- **Layer 1 — emergency fund:** 3–6 months (lean to 6+) of household essentials, in an accessible, inflation-beating MMF.
- **Layer 2 — insurance:** health (SHIF + private), life on breadwinners, home/motor, and last-expense cover — insure catastrophes, self-insure small stuff.
- **Layer 3 — prepare for specific shocks:** job loss (fund + skills + multiple incomes), medical, death (life cover + will), major repairs.
- Make it a **household habit:** build together, review cover as the family grows, talk through the "what ifs," and keep documents organised.
