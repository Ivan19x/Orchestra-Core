# Why We Spend: The Emotions Behind Money

You can know every budgeting rule and still overspend. Why? Because spending is rarely a maths problem — it is an **emotional** one. We buy to feel something: relief, status, comfort, belonging, control. Until you understand the *feelings* pulling your hand to M-Pesa, no budget will hold.

This lesson is the foundation of the whole *Psychology of Money* series: before you fix your spending, you have to understand your spending.

## Money decisions are made by feelings, then justified by logic

Picture it: you've had a rough week. You open your phone and order something you don't need. Your brain instantly supplies a "reason" — *I deserve it, it was on offer, I've been working hard*. But the decision came first, from the **feeling**; the reason was invented afterward to make it sound sensible.

This is normal human wiring, not a personal flaw. As Morgan Housel puts it in *The Psychology of Money*, people don't make money decisions on a spreadsheet — they make them at the dinner table, shaped by ego, history, and emotion. The skill is not to *stop* having feelings; it is to notice them before they spend your money.

## The common emotional drivers of spending

- **Stress and sadness** → "retail therapy." Spending gives a quick hit of relief or control when life feels out of control.
- **Boredom** → idle scrolling turns into idle buying.
- **Reward / "I deserve it"** → treating yourself after effort, which is fine in moderation but dangerous as a reflex.
- **Belonging** → buying to fit in with friends, colleagues, or a lifestyle.
- **Status** → buying to be *seen* as successful.
- **Fear of missing out (FOMO)** → "offer ends today," limited stock, everyone's doing it.

None of these are about the item. They are about the feeling the item promises.

## The Kenyan triggers worth naming

Spending pressure here often comes with a strong social face:
- **Image and status** — the pressure to *appear* to be doing well: the right phone, the right outfit, the right places, often broadcast on social media.
- **Events and obligations** — weddings, funerals, *harambees*, send-offs, and family gatherings carry real expectations to contribute and to show up well-dressed.
- **Black tax** — the genuine, ongoing pull to support extended family, which is noble but can quietly break a budget if it has no limit.
- **"Soft life" and online comparison** — a feed full of others' highlights makes your normal life feel like falling behind, and nudges spending to catch up.

Naming these out loud strips them of some power. A wedding contribution is real and often right — but it is a *choice* you can plan for, not an emergency that must blow your savings.

## How to spot your own triggers

For one week, before any non-essential purchase, pause and ask three questions:
1. **What am I feeling right now?** (stressed, bored, left out, proud, anxious)
2. **What am I really trying to buy?** (relief, status, belonging — not the object)
3. **Will this still feel worth it in three days?**

You'll quickly see a pattern — maybe you spend most when stressed, or after seeing certain people online. That pattern *is* the insight. You can't manage a trigger you can't see.

## Simple ways to manage the triggers (not kill the joy)

- **The 24-hour rule.** For any non-essential buy, wait a day. The feeling fades; the genuine wants survive.
- **Remove the easy path.** Log out of shopping apps; turn off one-tap checkout; unfollow accounts that reliably make you spend.
- **Feel the feeling another way.** Stressed? A walk, a call, rest — cheaper than a cart.
- **Pre-decide for obligations.** Set a yearly amount for events and black tax *in advance*, so each request is met from a plan, not panic.
- **Give yourself guilt-free spending.** Budgets that ban all fun fail. Allocate a "spend" slice so enjoyment is planned, not stolen from savings.

The goal is not to feel nothing. It is to let your *values*, not your *mood*, decide.

## Key takeaways

- Spending is mostly **emotional**; logic usually arrives *after* the decision to justify it.
- Common drivers: stress, boredom, reward, belonging, status, FOMO.
- Strong Kenyan triggers: **status/image, events, black tax, online comparison.**
- You can't manage a trigger you can't see — track your feelings before you spend.
- Manage triggers with the **24-hour rule**, removing easy paths, pre-deciding for obligations, and keeping planned fun money.
