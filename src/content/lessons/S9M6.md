# Life Insurance & Protecting Your Family

It's an uncomfortable question, but a vital one: **if your income stopped tomorrow — permanently — what would happen to the people who depend on you?** For anyone with a spouse, children, or family relying on what you earn, that's not a morbid thought; it's the most important planning question there is. Life insurance is how you answer it: a way to ensure that if the worst happens, your family is protected rather than plunged into crisis. This lesson cuts through the jargon so you can protect your family wisely and affordably.

> Series 2, Module 6 covers insurance broadly. This lesson goes deep on **life cover for families.**

## What life insurance actually does

Life insurance is a contract: you pay regular **premiums**, and if you die while covered, the insurer pays an agreed lump sum (the **sum assured**) to your chosen **beneficiaries**. That money replaces your lost income — so your family can keep paying rent, school fees, and daily costs, and isn't forced into debt, dependence, or pulling children out of school, at the worst possible time.

It exists for one reason: **to protect people who depend on you financially.** If no one relies on your income, you likely don't need it. The moment someone does — a child, a spouse who isn't earning, a dependent parent — it becomes essential.

In Kenya, life insurers are regulated by the **IRA**; deal only with licensed ones.

## The three main types — and which to choose

This is where people get confused and over-sold. Three common forms:

| Type | What it is | Cost | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Term life** | Pure cover for a set period (e.g. 10–20 yrs). Pays out only if you die within the term. | **Low** | **Best value for most families** |
| **Whole life** | Lifelong cover with a savings/cash component. | High | Cover + savings bundled — usually costlier |
| **Endowment** | Pays out on death *or* at the end of a term (a savings-plus-cover product). | High | Mixes insurance and saving |

**For most families, term life is the smart choice.** It's pure protection at a low cost — you get a large cover amount for a small premium, precisely covering the years your family is most vulnerable (while children are young and dependent). Whole life and endowment bundle in a savings element, which sounds appealing but is usually a more expensive, less flexible way to both insure and save.

The widely-taught principle: **"buy term and invest the difference."** Take cheap term cover for protection, and invest the money you save (versus pricier policies) separately in an MMF, SACCO or bonds — where it grows better and stays accessible. This usually beats the bundled savings of whole-life/endowment products.

## How much cover do you need?

A common guideline: enough to **replace your income for the years your family needs it** — often **5–10× your annual income**, adjusted for your situation. Think about what the payout must do:
- Cover **living costs** for your dependants for several years.
- Clear major **debts** (so your family doesn't inherit them).
- Fund **children's education** to completion (a huge cost — Series 9, Module 3).
- Provide a buffer while the family adjusts.

Better slightly more cover than too little — but don't over-insure beyond what your dependants would genuinely need, which just wastes premium.

## Why young parents especially can't skip it

It feels least necessary exactly when it matters most. Young parents typically have:
- **The most dependence on their income** — young children with many years of costs ahead.
- **The least savings** to fall back on.
- **The lowest premiums** — life cover is *cheapest when you're young and healthy*, and only gets pricier with age.

So young parents get the **most protection for the least money** — and have the most to lose by going without. Delaying "until I can afford it" usually means paying more later for the same cover, while leaving the family exposed in between.

## A few practical pointers

- **Name and update your beneficiaries** clearly — and note that naming beneficiaries on policies (and on your pension and SACCO) helps that money reach your family quickly, bypassing disputes (Series 9, Module 7).
- **Buy enough, keep it simple** — a solid term policy beats a fancy one you can't afford to maintain.
- **Read the exclusions** and disclose honestly, so the claim is paid when needed.
- **Review it** as life changes — more children, a mortgage, higher income all change how much cover you need.

## Key takeaways

- Life insurance **replaces your income** to protect dependants if you die — essential the moment someone relies on you.
- **Term life is best value for most families** — large cover, low cost, for the vulnerable years.
- Prefer **"buy term and invest the difference"** over bundled whole-life/endowment savings.
- Size cover to **replace income for years + clear debts + fund education** (often ~5–10× annual income).
- **Young parents need it most and pay least** — cover is cheapest when young; name your beneficiaries and review as life changes.
