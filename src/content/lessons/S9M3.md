# Saving for Your Children's Education

For most Kenyan families, educating children is the single largest planned expense of their lives — and unlike a surprise, it's completely predictable. You know, the day a child is born, that fees are coming. That predictability is a gift: it means education is something you can *plan and save for* calmly, instead of scrambling each term with panic loans. This lesson shows the real costs and how to be ready for them.

## The real cost of education in Kenya (2026)

Costs vary hugely by the path you choose, but rough current figures help you plan:

**Secondary school (annual):**
- **Day school** — largely government-funded (free day secondary, capitation ~KES 22,000/learner); parents mainly cover **lunch** and incidentals.
- **County / extra-county boarding** — roughly **KES 40,000–45,000** a year.
- **National / top boarding** — around **KES 53,000–76,000** a year.
- **Private schools** — can be far higher, into the hundreds of thousands.

**University (public, new funding model from 2025):** under the Student-Centred Funding Model, tuition was reduced and is **means-tested** — e.g. an arts degree might be roughly **KES 7,500–30,000 per semester**, professional courses (medicine, etc.) much more — topped up by **government scholarships and HELB loans** based on need. Private universities and living costs add substantially more.

Two takeaways: boarding and university are **lumpy, five- and six-figure** commitments; and even "free" or subsidised paths carry real costs (lunch, uniforms, transport, upkeep, exams).

## Why education planning is different

- **It's lumpy.** Fees land in big chunks each term/semester, not monthly — exactly the pattern that breaks unprepared budgets.
- **It's long-horizon and certain.** You have *years* of warning, which is perfect for saving early and letting compounding help.
- **It inflates.** School fees tend to rise over time, so plan for tomorrow's prices, not today's.
- **Missing it is costly.** Families caught short take fee loans at high rates, or disrupt a child's schooling — both avoidable with planning.

## The tool: an education sinking fund

The single best move is a dedicated **education sinking fund** — a separate pot you feed monthly so each term's fees are already waiting.

> **How to size it.** Estimate the annual cost, divide by 12, save that slice. Boarding fees of ~KES 60,000/year ÷ 12 = **KES 5,000/month** into a separate fund. When the term opens, the money is there — no loan, no stress.

Keep this pot **separate** from everyday money (so it isn't spent) but **accessible and earning** — a money market fund is ideal: it beats inflation (~7.6% net vs ~6.7%), compounds daily, and you can withdraw in days when fees are due. For very long horizons (a newborn's university, 18 years away), longer-term investments can play a role too.

## Start early — time does the heavy lifting

Because money compounds, starting when a child is *young* dramatically reduces what you must save. Saving a modest amount monthly from birth, in a fund that grows, can cover what would otherwise be a frightening lump sum — because the early shillings have many years to grow (Series 8, Module 2). Wait until secondary school looms, and you must save far more, far faster, with no time for growth to help. **The cheapest time to start an education fund is the year the child is born.**

## Other routes (use carefully)

- **Education-specific plans** from insurers/unit trusts — structured education savings, sometimes with a small insurance element. Read the fees and terms critically; a plain MMF is often simpler and cheaper, but disciplined parents like the commitment a plan enforces.
- **SACCO savings** — build deposits that both grow and unlock a cheap loan for a fee shortfall.
- **HELB and bursaries** — for university, government loans and county/constituency bursaries reduce what families pay (Series 5, Module 5). Plan to *use* these, not to be surprised by the gap they leave.
- **Avoid high-interest fee loans** as the default — they're the expensive symptom of not having planned.

## Key takeaways

- Education is most families' **biggest planned expense** — but it's predictable, so it's plannable.
- Real costs vary: secondary boarding ~**KES 40,000–76,000/year**; university now **means-tested** plus HELB/scholarships — all still lumpy.
- Build a **dedicated education sinking fund** (annual cost ÷ 12), kept separate but in an **inflation-beating, accessible MMF.**
- **Start early** — compounding means the year a child is born is the cheapest time to begin.
- Use HELB, bursaries and SACCO loans as planned support — not high-interest fee loans as a surprise fix.
