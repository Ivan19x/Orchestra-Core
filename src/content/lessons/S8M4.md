# SACCOs as an Investment

Most Kenyans think of a SACCO as a place to *save* and *borrow*. Fewer see it for what it can also be: a genuine **investment** — one that, for disciplined members, can return more than almost any other low-to-medium-risk option available to an ordinary person. The combination of a strong annual **dividend**, a deposit **rebate**, and access to **cheap loans** makes the SACCO a quiet wealth engine. This lesson looks at the SACCO purely through an investor's lens.

> The mechanics (BOSA/FOSA, share capital vs deposits, SASRA, the loan multiplier) are covered in Series 4, Module 3. Here we focus on returns and compounding.

## Two returns from one membership

As an investor, a SACCO pays you two ways every year:
1. **Dividend on share capital** — your return on *ownership*. At top SACCOs this has recently been **15–20%+** (e.g. Tower ~20%, Police ~17%, Stima ~16% on share capital in FY2025).
2. **Rebate (interest) on deposits** — your return on *savings*, recently around **10–13%**.

Put those next to a bank savings account paying low single digits, and the SACCO's appeal as an investment is obvious. (These are declared yearly at the AGM and vary — treat them as recent examples, not guarantees.)

## The compounding power of reinvested dividends

Here's where it becomes a serious wealth tool. If, instead of spending your annual dividend, you **plough it back** into more share capital, next year's dividend is calculated on a bigger base — and the snowball builds (compound interest, Series 8, Module 2). A member who consistently:
- **contributes monthly** to share capital and deposits, and
- **reinvests dividends** year after year,

can build a substantial holding over a decade or two — earning strong returns *and* steadily increasing their cheap-loan capacity. Patience plus reinvestment is the whole strategy.

## The hidden return: cheap credit

There's a return that doesn't show up as a percentage: **access to loans at roughly 1% per month on a reducing balance, up to ~3x your deposits.** That's far cheaper than mobile loans (which annualise past 100%) or many bank loans. When you eventually borrow — for a plot, a business, fees — the *money you save* on interest versus the alternatives is a real, large return on having built up your SACCO. The SACCO both grows your money *and* makes your future borrowing cheap.

## An honest comparison: SACCO vs MMF vs bank

No single tool is "best" — they do different jobs:

| | **SACCO** | **Money market fund** | **Bank savings** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Return (2026)** | High (dividend 15–20%+, rebate 10–13%) | ~9% gross (~7.6% net) | Low single digits |
| **Access** | Locked-in; backs loans | 1–3 days | Instant |
| **Best for** | Long-term wealth + cheap credit | Emergency fund, flexible savings | Day-to-day spending |
| **Risk** | Low–moderate (governance risk) | Low | Low |

The smart play uses **both** a SACCO and an MMF: the **MMF holds your emergency fund** (because it's quickly accessible) and flexible savings, while the **SACCO is your long-term wealth-and-credit engine** (because the lock-in enforces discipline and the returns are higher). The bank account is just your spending hub.

## The risks to respect

The SACCO is powerful but not risk-free as an investment:
- **Governance risk** — mismanagement or fraud has hurt some SACCOs (the KUSCCO troubles are a caution). Favour well-run, **SASRA-licensed** SACCOs with clean track records.
- **Liquidity** — your money is genuinely locked in, so a SACCO shouldn't hold money you might need fast.
- **Concentration** — don't put *everything* in one SACCO; balance with more liquid assets.

Used wisely, though, a good SACCO is one of the best wealth tools an ordinary Kenyan has.

## Key takeaways

- A SACCO can be a genuine **investment**, not just a savings club.
- It pays **two returns** — a share dividend (15–20%+) and a deposit rebate (10–13%) — far above bank savings.
- **Reinvesting dividends** compounds your holding into serious wealth over years.
- A hidden return is **cheap credit** (~1%/month, ~3x deposits) versus brutal mobile loans.
- Use a **SACCO + MMF together**: MMF for the accessible emergency fund, SACCO for long-term wealth — while respecting governance and liquidity risk.
