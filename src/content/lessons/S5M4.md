# Your First Job or Hustle as a Young Person

Your first real income — from a first job, an internship, or a hustle that finally takes off — is a turning point. Handle it well and you build habits and a head start that compound for decades. Handle it badly and "lifestyle" swallows it the moment it arrives, leaving you no further ahead at 25 than at 18. The amount is usually small and often irregular; the *habits* you set now are what matter. This lesson shows how to start your earning life on the right foot.

## The first-income trap: lifestyle inflation, early

The most common mistake is the simplest: the money comes in, and your spending instantly rises to swallow it. New income feels like permission to upgrade everything — better phone, eating out, nights out. Six months later the income is bigger but the savings are still zero. **Decide what to do with your money before it arrives**, so it goes to a plan, not to whatever feels good in the moment.

## Manage irregular income like a pro

Early income is often **irregular** — a good hustle month, a slow one, a gig here and there. The trick the experienced use:
- **Budget on a low, cautious figure** — roughly your typical *lean* month — and treat anything above it as a bonus.
- **Save the good months hard.** When a big payment lands, save a large slice immediately, before lifestyle absorbs it. The surplus from good months carries you through slow ones.
- **Build a buffer first.** Even a small emergency fund smooths the gaps so a slow month doesn't force a mobile loan (your worst enemy — Module 3).

This "feast and famine" discipline is the core skill of every freelancer, hustler and entrepreneur.

## Pay yourself first — from the very first shilling

The single habit that will set you apart: **save a slice of every payment the moment it arrives.** Even 10–20%. Started at 18–22, this habit — feeding an MMF or SACCO — has decades to compound (Series 8, Module 2), which is why early savers can end up ahead of later, bigger earners. Automate it if you can. Future-you will be astonished at what early, small, consistent saving became.

## Your first taxes: get a KRA PIN

As you enter the working world, tax becomes part of life. The essentials for a young earner:
- **Get a KRA PIN.** It's free, done online via iTax, and you'll need it for almost everything — a formal job, a bank/CDS account, a business, even some transactions. Get it early.
- **If employed:** your employer deducts **PAYE** and the statutory cuts from your salary (Series 2, Module 1) and remits them. You should still **file your annual return** on iTax — it's required even when your employer has deducted everything, and it's free.
- **If hustling/self-employed:** your income is still taxable. Keep simple records of what you earn, and file. Small businesses may fall under **Turnover Tax** as they grow (Series 4, Module 6).
- **Don't fear it.** Tax sounds intimidating but the basics (get a PIN, file once a year) are simple once you've done them once.

## Build the foundations now

Use your first earning years to put the basics in place — they're far easier to start young:
- **Open the right accounts** (Series 5, Module 6): a low-fee bank account, an MMF for savings, maybe a SACCO.
- **Start the emergency fund.**
- **Stay off the debt traps** (Module 3) — no mobile-loan habit, no betting.
- **Invest in your earning power.** At this stage, the highest-return "investment" is often *you* — skills, qualifications, and a good reputation that raise your income for life.

## Key takeaways

- The first-income trap is **instant lifestyle inflation** — decide your plan *before* the money arrives.
- For **irregular income**, budget on a cautious low figure, **save the good months hard**, and keep a buffer.
- **Pay yourself first** from the very first shilling — early saving compounds for decades.
- **Get a KRA PIN early** and file your annual return (required even if your employer deducts PAYE).
- Use early years to build foundations: right accounts, emergency fund, no debt traps, and **investing in your own skills.**
