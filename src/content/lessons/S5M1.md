# Your First Money: Pocket Money, Gifts & Earning Young

The first money you ever control is small — a KES 200 gift from an aunt, lunch money you didn't spend, KES 500 from a weekend hustle. It feels too little to matter. Here is the secret nobody tells you: **the amount doesn't matter yet — the habit does.** A teenager who learns to handle KES 500 well becomes an adult who handles KES 500,000 well. One who blows every shilling that arrives becomes an adult who does the same with a salary.

This lesson is about starting those habits early, while the stakes are low and mistakes are cheap.

## Where your first money comes from

For most young Kenyans, early money arrives as:
- **Pocket money or allowance** from parents.
- **Gifts** — birthdays, holidays, *Christmas*, from family.
- **Early earning** — small hustles: helping in a family shop, selling snacks or airtime at school, washing cars, simple online tasks, holiday work, fixing phones, tutoring younger students.

All three are practice money. The goal is not to get rich at 16 — it is to learn the moves you'll use for life.

## The one habit that changes your future: save a slice of everything

Make this a personal law, starting now: **whenever any money reaches you, a portion goes to savings before you spend a shilling.** Even 10% of a KES 200 gift is KES 20. The number is tiny; the *muscle* you are building is enormous.

This is the famous "pay yourself first" rule (from *The Richest Man in Babylon*), and learning it at 16 instead of 30 is a genuine head start. Why? Because of **time**. Money saved young has decades to grow on itself (you'll meet this as compound interest later). The teenager who saves a little, consistently, can end up far ahead of someone who starts saving large amounts much later.

## Give every shilling a job

A simple way to handle any money you receive — split it into three jobs:
- **Save** — a slice you don't touch (your growing pot).
- **Spend** — guilt-free money for what you enjoy now.
- **Goal** — money set aside for something specific you want.

Even with KES 500, that might be KES 100 save, KES 250 spend, KES 150 toward a goal. Deciding *before* the money arrives is the whole skill.

## Set a real goal and feel the win

Saving is boring without a target. Pick something you actually want — a pair of shoes, a bigger phone, a bike, course fees — find its price, and work out how long saving your slice will take. ("KES 4,000 shoes, saving KES 200 a week = 20 weeks.") Watching the pot grow toward a real goal is how saving stops feeling like punishment and starts feeling like power. Hitting that first self-funded goal teaches a lesson no lecture can: **you can make money do what you want, if you plan.**

## Earning young: the real prize is the lesson

When you earn your own money — even KES 300 from a small hustle — something clicks that gifted money never teaches: you learn what money *costs* in effort. That respect for money is priceless. Early hustles also teach you to spot a need, serve a customer, and keep simple records — the seeds of every business. Don't despise small earnings; treasure what they teach.

## A few traps to dodge early

- **Spending the moment money arrives** — the fastest way to stay broke at any age.
- **Copying friends' spending** — their money is not your money; their choices needn't be yours.
- **Lending to friends carelessly** — be generous, but learn early that money and friendship mix badly when unpaid.

## Key takeaways

- With your first money, **the habit matters far more than the amount.**
- **Save a slice of everything** the moment it arrives — pay yourself first.
- Give money jobs: **save, spend, goal** — decide before it arrives.
- Saving toward a **real goal** turns discipline into a winnable game.
- **Earning young** teaches the true value of money better than any gift.
