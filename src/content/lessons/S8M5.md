# The Stock Market & NSE

The stock market is where ordinary money meets real business ownership — and where, over the long run, patient investors have built serious wealth. It's also where the impatient and the hype-driven lose money fastest. The difference isn't luck; it's understanding what you're actually doing. This lesson is about shares *as an investment* — how they grow your money, the risks to respect, and how a beginner can do it sensibly.

> Series 4, Module 4 covers the *mechanics* (CDS account, brokers, how to place an order). This lesson is about the *investing thinking*.

## What you're really buying

A **share** is a slice of ownership in a real company. Buy Safaricom shares and you own a tiny piece of Safaricom — entitled to a share of its profits and its future growth. You're not betting on a number; you're becoming a part-owner of a business. That framing changes everything: good investors think like **owners of businesses**, not like gamblers on prices.

## How shares make you money — two ways

1. **Dividends** — your share of the company's profits, paid out (often yearly or half-yearly). A mature, profitable company pays steady dividends — passive income for simply holding the share.
2. **Capital growth** — if the company grows and becomes more valuable, its share price rises, so your holding is worth more than you paid. (It can also *fall* — that's the risk.)

Long-term stock returns come from **both**: years of dividends collected (and reinvested to compound), plus gradual price appreciation. The wealth is in *time in the market*, not clever timing.

## The risk you must respect

Shares are higher-risk than savings products, and pretending otherwise gets people hurt:
- **Prices fall as well as rise** — sometimes for years. You can lose money, even on a big-name company.
- **Volatility is normal.** The market swings up and down constantly; that's the price of admission for higher long-term returns. Panic-selling in a dip is how investors lock in losses.
- **It's long-term money only.** Shares suit money you can leave for **5+ years** and ride out the swings. Money you need next year does not belong here — that's what your MMF and emergency fund are for (Series 8, Module 1).

The honest rule: higher potential return (shares) comes with higher risk (volatility and possible loss). No exceptions.

## Diversification: the one free lunch

Putting everything into a single share — because it's popular, or a friend's "tip" — is the classic beginner mistake. If that one company stumbles, so does your whole investment. **Diversification** — spreading across several companies and sectors — is the closest thing investing has to a free lunch: it reduces your risk without necessarily reducing your expected return, because the winners cushion the losers. Never let one stock be your whole portfolio.

## The simpler path for beginners: collective options

Picking individual winning stocks is genuinely hard — even professionals often fail at it. The good news: you don't have to. **Collective investment vehicles** let you buy a diversified basket in one purchase:
- **Unit trusts / equity funds** — a manager pools investors' money and buys a spread of shares for you.
- **Index-style options** — track a whole market index (like the NSE) rather than betting on single companies.
- **REITs** (e.g. listed property funds) — exposure to real estate without buying property.

For most beginners, a **diversified collective vehicle is wiser than hand-picking stocks** — instant diversification, professional management, and far less chance of a single bad pick sinking you.

## A sensible way to invest in shares

- **First things first.** Only invest money you won't need for years — *after* clearing bad debt and building an emergency fund.
- **Start with diversification** — a collective fund, or several solid companies, not one "hot" stock.
- **Invest regularly** (a fixed amount monthly), which smooths out the highs and lows over time.
- **Reinvest dividends** to compound.
- **Think in years, ignore the noise.** Don't check prices daily or react to headlines and tips (Series 3, Module 4; Series 6, Module 5).
- **Keep shares as one part** of a balanced plan that also includes safer assets (MMF, SACCO, bonds).

## Key takeaways

- A share is **real ownership** in a business — invest like an owner, not a gambler.
- Shares pay via **dividends + capital growth**, captured over the **long term**.
- They carry **real risk and volatility** — only money you can leave for 5+ years belongs here.
- **Diversify** — never let one stock be your whole portfolio (the free lunch of investing).
- For most beginners, a **diversified collective fund beats picking individual stocks.**
