# Net Worth: Knowing What You're Worth

Your salary tells you what you *earn*. Your net worth tells you what you're actually *worth* — and the two are very different. Someone earning KES 200,000 a month with nothing saved and a pile of loans can be worth *less* than someone earning KES 40,000 who has quietly built savings and a plot. Net worth is the single number that captures your true financial position — the scoreboard of your whole money life. This final lesson of *Money Basics* shows you how to calculate it, track it, and grow it.

## What net worth is

Net worth is a beautifully simple formula:

> **Net worth = what you OWN (assets) − what you OWE (liabilities)**

- **Assets** are everything of value you own — savings, investments, property, things that put money in your pocket or could be sold.
- **Liabilities** are everything you owe — loans, debts, anything that takes money out of your pocket.

Subtract the second from the first, and you have your net worth. It can be positive (you own more than you owe — good) or negative (you owe more than you own — common when young, but a sign to fix).

## How to calculate yours: the Kenyan version

List your **assets** (current realistic value):
- Cash, bank balances, **M-Pesa/M-Shwari**
- **Money market fund** balance
- **SACCO** deposits and share capital
- **Shares** (NSE), **bonds**
- **Land or property** you own (market value)
- A business you own, valuable equipment
- (Be honest — value things at what you'd actually get, not what you paid)

List your **liabilities** (what you owe today):
- **Bank/SACCO loans** (outstanding balance)
- **Mobile loans, Fuliza**
- **HELB** balance
- Hire-purchase, any other debts
- Unpaid bills

> **Worked example.** Assets: MMF KES 80,000 + SACCO KES 120,000 + M-Pesa KES 10,000 + a plot worth KES 600,000 = **KES 810,000**. Liabilities: SACCO loan KES 200,000 + HELB KES 90,000 + Fuliza KES 5,000 = **KES 295,000**. **Net worth = 810,000 − 295,000 = KES 515,000.**

That single number is a clearer picture of your finances than any payslip.

## Why net worth beats income as a measure of wealth

This is the key insight: **wealth is what you keep, not what you earn.** A high income that all gets spent (and borrowed against) builds no wealth — it just passes through. A modest income, partly saved and invested, *accumulates* into real net worth over time. That's why:
- A flashy high-earner can have a **low or negative** net worth (financed car, big rent, loans, nothing saved).
- A quiet modest-earner can have a **strong** net worth (savings, SACCO, a plot, little debt).

Income is the *flow*; net worth is the *result*. The whole point of budgeting, saving, avoiding bad debt, and investing — everything this series taught — is ultimately to **grow your net worth.** It's the scoreboard that tells you if it's all working.

## Track it over time — the real value

A single net-worth figure is useful; **tracking it over time** is powerful. Calculate it once, then again every few months or once a year. The *direction* is what matters:
- **Rising net worth** = you're winning — assets growing, debts shrinking, the system working.
- **Flat or falling** = a signal to look at your spending, saving, or debt, even if your income is fine.

Watching the number climb is also genuinely motivating — it turns abstract "being good with money" into a concrete score you can improve. Many people find that simply *measuring* their net worth makes them manage money better, because what gets measured gets managed.

## How to grow your net worth

It's the whole curriculum in one sentence: **grow your assets and shrink your liabilities.**
- **Increase assets** — save and invest consistently (MMF, SACCO, bonds, property), buy income-producing assets (Series 3, Module 3).
- **Decrease liabilities** — pay down debt, especially expensive bad debt, and avoid new bad debt.
- **Grow the gap** — earn more, keep lifestyle below income, and channel the difference into assets (Series 3, Module 7).

Every good money habit moves this one number in the right direction.

## Key takeaways

- **Net worth = assets (what you own) − liabilities (what you owe)** — the true scoreboard of your finances.
- Calculate yours by **listing realistic asset values** (MMF, SACCO, property, shares) and **all debts** (loans, Fuliza, HELB).
- **Wealth is what you keep, not what you earn** — net worth beats income as a measure (high earners can be worth little; modest earners, a lot).
- **Track it over time** — the *direction* (rising) matters most, and measuring it improves behaviour.
- Grow it by **increasing assets, shrinking liabilities, and widening the gap** — the goal of every money habit.
