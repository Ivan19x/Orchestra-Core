# Your First Salary & Understanding Your Payslip

You are offered a job at "fifty thousand a month." Payday comes, your phone buzzes, and the M-Pesa message says something closer to **KES 39,000**. Nobody stole the difference. It went to four deductions that every Kenyan in formal employment pays — and understanding them is the difference between feeling cheated and feeling in control.

This lesson walks down a real payslip, line by line, so you know exactly where your money goes and what each cut actually buys you.

## Gross pay vs net pay

- **Gross pay** is the headline figure — the salary you negotiated, before anything is taken out.
- **Net pay** (often called "take-home") is what actually lands in your account after deductions.

The journey from gross to net passes through four mandatory deductions. Three of them (NSSF, SHIF and the Housing Levy) are taken off *first* and, helpfully, they reduce the income that income tax is then calculated on.

## The four deductions, in order

### 1. NSSF — your retirement savings
The **National Social Security Fund** is forced retirement saving. Since the rates were enhanced in **February 2025**, you contribute **6% of your pensionable pay**, and your employer adds a matching 6% on top (so it is partly "free" money from your employer).

It is split into two tiers:
- **Tier I** covers the first **KES 8,000** of salary → 6% = **KES 480**.
- **Tier II** covers salary from KES 8,000 up to an upper limit of **KES 72,000** → up to **KES 3,840**.

So an employee earning KES 50,000 pays `480 + (6% of (50,000 − 8,000)) = 480 + 2,520 =` **KES 3,000**. Someone earning KES 72,000 or more pays the maximum of **KES 4,320**. This money is not gone — it is *yours*, saved for retirement, and your employer matched it.

### 2. SHIF — your health cover
The **Social Health Insurance Fund** replaced the old NHIF in **October 2024**. It is **2.75% of your gross pay**, with no upper cap. On KES 50,000 that is **KES 1,375** a month. In return you are enrolled in the national health scheme (under the Social Health Authority, SHA) that helps cover hospital and outpatient treatment.

### 3. Housing Levy (AHL) — the newest cut
The **Affordable Housing Levy** has applied since **March 2024**. It is **1.5% of gross pay** from you, with your employer adding another **1.5%**. On KES 50,000 that is **KES 750**. It funds the government's affordable-housing programme.

### 4. PAYE — income tax
**Pay As You Earn** is the income tax your employer deducts and sends to KRA on your behalf. Crucially, it is calculated on your pay *after* NSSF, SHIF and the Housing Levy are removed (those three are now allowable deductions, since late 2024).

Kenya's monthly tax bands (as of 2026):

| Monthly taxable pay (KES) | Rate |
|---|---|
| First 24,000 | 10% |
| 24,001 – 32,333 | 25% |
| 32,334 – 500,000 | 30% |
| 500,001 – 800,000 | 32.5% |
| Above 800,000 | 35% |

Then everyone gets a **personal relief of KES 2,400 per month** subtracted from the tax. That relief is exactly 10% of KES 24,000 — which is why, in practice, **the first KES 24,000 of taxable pay is effectively tax-free**.

## A full worked payslip — KES 50,000 gross

| Line | Amount (KES) |
|---|---|
| Gross pay | 50,000 |
| − NSSF | 3,000 |
| − SHIF (2.75%) | 1,375 |
| − Housing Levy (1.5%) | 750 |
| **Taxable pay** | **44,875** |
| PAYE before relief | 8,246 |
| − Personal relief | (2,400) |
| **PAYE payable** | **5,846** |
| **Net (take-home)** | **≈ 39,029** |

How the PAYE was built: 10% of the first 24,000 (2,400) + 25% of the next 8,333 (2,083) + 30% of the remaining 12,542 (3,763) = 8,246, less the 2,400 relief = **5,846**.

So "fifty thousand" really means **about KES 39,000** in your hand — and roughly KES 3,000 of the difference (NSSF) is still your own savings.

## What each deduction actually buys you

It is easy to see deductions as theft. Reframe them:
- **NSSF** → your future self's money (and your employer matched it).
- **SHIF** → health cover so one hospital visit doesn't wipe out your savings.
- **Housing Levy** → the national housing fund.
- **PAYE** → the roads, schools and services everyone uses.

Only PAYE truly "leaves" you. The rest is either saving or insurance.

## How to read and check your own payslip

- Confirm the gross matches your contract.
- Check NSSF, SHIF (2.75%) and AHL (1.5%) are calculated correctly.
- At year-end your employer gives you a **P9 form** — the summary you use to file your annual return on **KRA's iTax** (filing is free and mandatory, even if your employer already deducted everything).
- If a number looks wrong, ask HR for the breakdown. You have a right to understand every line.

## Key takeaways

- **Gross is the promise; net is the reality.** Always budget on net pay.
- Four deductions: **NSSF (savings), SHIF (health), Housing Levy, PAYE (tax)** — in that order.
- NSSF and SHIF/AHL are taken *before* tax, lowering the income PAYE is charged on.
- The **first KES 24,000** of taxable pay is effectively tax-free thanks to the KES 2,400 relief.
- A KES 50,000 salary nets roughly **KES 39,000** today — but about KES 3,000 of the gap is your own retirement savings.
