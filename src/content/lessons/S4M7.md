# Land & Property in Kenya

Land is the dream investment for many Kenyans — and the source of some of the most devastating financial losses, because land fraud is rampant. Buy well and you own an appreciating asset for life; buy carelessly and you can lose your entire savings to a fake title, a double-sold plot, or a "seller" who never owned the land. The difference is **due diligence**: knowing how to verify, the correct steps, and the real costs. This lesson is your safety guide to buying land and property in Kenya.

## Start with the title: types and searches

A **title deed** is the legal proof of land ownership. Before *anything*, you must confirm the seller genuinely owns the land and that it's free of problems.

**Do a land search.** Through the Ministry of Lands' **Ardhisasa** platform (now largely digital), you can run an **official title search** for about **KES 500**, which reveals:
- The **true registered owner** (does it match your "seller"?).
- Any **caveats, charges, or disputes** (e.g. the land used as loan security, or a court dispute).
- The land's **size, location, and registration details.**

Never proceed on a seller's word or a photocopied title — run the search yourself (or via your lawyer). This single step prevents most land fraud.

**Know the title types** — freehold (you own it outright, indefinitely) vs leasehold (you hold it for a term, e.g. 99 years, from the government or another owner). Understand which you're buying and the lease terms if leasehold.

## The steps to buy safely

A safe purchase follows a clear process — don't skip steps to "save time":
1. **Identify the land and seller**, and get the title details.
2. **Do an official search** (Ardhisasa) — confirm ownership and that it's clean.
3. **Engage a lawyer (advocate).** Conveyancing is genuinely worth the fee — they verify documents, draft the sale agreement, and protect you. Buying land without a lawyer to save money is a classic, costly mistake.
4. **Sign a sale agreement**, usually paying a deposit (commonly ~10%), with the balance on completion.
5. **For agricultural land, get Land Control Board (LCB) consent** (~KES 1,000) — required to transfer ag land.
6. **Conduct due diligence** — a physical site visit, confirm boundaries (a surveyor), check for squatters, and confirm there are no other claimants. Never buy land you haven't physically visited.
7. **Pay stamp duty and transfer the title** through Ardhisasa (stamp duty now via **Ardhipay**), so the title is registered in *your* name.
8. **Confirm the transfer** with a fresh search showing you as the owner.

## The real costs (budget for these)

The price of the land is not the only cost. Total **closing costs typically run 7%–11%** of the purchase price:
- **Stamp duty** — **4% of value for urban** property, **2% for rural** land (and **6% for commercial**). This is the big one.
- **Legal/conveyancing fees** — about **1–2%** (plus 16% VAT).
- **Valuation fees** — ~0.25–1% (a government valuer assesses the value for stamp duty).
- **Search and registration fees** — search ~KES 500, transfer levy ~KES 5,000, LCB consent ~KES 1,000 (ag land).

> On a KES 5 million urban plot, budget roughly **KES 350,000–550,000** in closing costs on top of the price. Factor this in *before* you commit — many buyers are caught short.

## The classic land scams — and how to dodge them

Kenyan land fraud is creative and common. The big ones:
- **Selling land they don't own** — fraudsters with fake or stolen titles. **Defence:** the official Ardhisasa search confirms the real owner.
- **Double-selling** — selling the same plot to several buyers. **Defence:** search just before paying, and register the transfer *immediately*.
- **Fake titles** — forged documents. **Defence:** verify through Ardhisasa, not the paper handed to you.
- **Selling public/riparian/disputed land**, or land with squatters or court cases. **Defence:** search (caveats show), site visit, and a lawyer's diligence.
- **"Too good to be true" prices** — wildly cheap land is a red flag for a problem.

The umbrella defence: **verify independently, use a lawyer, visit the land, and never pay large sums on trust or in a hurry.** Fraudsters rely on buyers being rushed and trusting; slow, verified buying defeats them.

## Key takeaways

- **Always do an official Ardhisasa title search (~KES 500)** to confirm the real owner and any caveats — before paying anything.
- **Use a lawyer** for conveyancing; skipping one to save money is a classic costly mistake.
- Follow the **steps**: search → lawyer → sale agreement → LCB consent (ag) → due diligence/site visit → pay stamp duty → transfer title → confirm.
- Budget **7–11% closing costs** — stamp duty is **4% urban / 2% rural / 6% commercial.**
- Beat land scams (fake titles, double-selling) by **verifying independently, visiting the land, and never paying in a rush.**
