# Retirement Planning & Pensions

Retirement feels impossibly far away when you're young — which is exactly why most people don't plan for it, and exactly why so many older Kenyans struggle. Here's the hard truth: a day will come when you can't or don't want to work, and your income must come from what you built beforehand. The good news: retirement is the *most predictable* financial event of your life — you know it's coming — and Kenya offers tax-advantaged ways to prepare. This lesson shows how, and why starting now matters more than how much you save.

## Why retirement planning can't wait

You will, one day, stop earning a salary — through age, choice, or circumstance. At that point you need an income to live on for potentially decades. It can come from a pension, your investments, assets like rentals, or family — and relying on family alone is a fragile, often unfair plan. The earlier you build your *own* retirement pot, the easier it is, because of **compounding** (Series 8, Module 2): money saved at 25 has 35 years to grow; money saved at 50 has 15. Starting early can mean the difference between a comfortable retirement from modest savings and a stressful one from frantic late saving. Retirement planning isn't an "old person" topic — it's a *young* person's superpower.

## The three pillars of retirement income in Kenya

### 1. NSSF (the mandatory base)
The **National Social Security Fund** is compulsory retirement saving for employees (Series 2, Module 1). Since the 2024–25 enhancements, you contribute **6% of pensionable pay**, matched by your employer, building a pot you access in retirement. It's a foundation — but on its own, NSSF is **unlikely to be enough** to maintain your lifestyle. Treat it as a base to build on, not the whole plan.

### 2. Employer / occupational pension schemes
Many formal employers offer a **pension or provident fund** — you and the employer contribute, often more generously than NSSF. If your employer offers one, **join and contribute** (it's often partly "free" employer money), and understand its terms. These schemes are regulated by the **RBA (Retirement Benefits Authority)**.

### 3. Personal pension plans (the part you control)
Anyone — employed, self-employed, or hustling — can open a **personal pension plan** with a provider, and contribute on their own terms. This is essential if you're self-employed (no employer scheme) or want to top up beyond NSSF/employer cover. It's the pillar fully in *your* control.

## The tax breaks make pensions powerful

Pensions aren't just saving — they come with **genuine tax advantages** the government uses to encourage retirement saving:
- **Contributions are tax-deductible** up to **KES 20,000 per month (KES 240,000 a year)** — meaning that portion of your income isn't taxed now, so you save *and* cut your tax bill.
- **Pension income in retirement is tax-free** up to about **KES 300,000 a year (KES 25,000/month).**
- **Lump-sum withdrawals** get tax-free treatment too (a tax-free amount per year of membership, up to a cap).

These breaks make a pension one of the few places your money grows **tax-advantaged** — a real edge over saving the same money in a fully-taxed account. For higher earners especially, the upfront tax relief is significant.

## How much should you save for retirement?

There's no single number, but useful guides:
- A common target is to save around **10–15% of your income** toward retirement throughout your working life (including NSSF and employer contributions).
- Think in terms of **replacing your income** — you'll want enough invested to generate a livable income for decades without working.
- **The earlier you start, the lower the percentage you need**, because compounding does more of the work. Wait, and you must save a *much* higher share to catch up (Series 8, Module 2).

Don't be paralysed by the big final number — start with a sustainable percentage now and increase it as your income grows (banking raises into retirement, Series 3, Module 7).

## A practical retirement plan

- **Start now**, however small — time is the biggest factor.
- **Maximise "free" money** — join your employer scheme and capture any match; NSSF is automatic.
- **Open a personal pension** if self-employed or to top up — and use the **tax relief** (up to KES 20,000/month).
- **Don't rely on NSSF alone** — it's a base, not a full plan.
- **Invest retirement money for growth** while you're young (it's long-term money — shares/balanced funds), shifting to safer assets as retirement nears (Series 8, Module 8).
- **Don't raid it early** — the power is in leaving it to compound for decades.

## Key takeaways

- You **will** stop earning one day — retirement is predictable, so plan for it, and **start young** to let compounding work.
- Three pillars: **NSSF** (mandatory base, not enough alone), **employer pension** (join, capture the match), and a **personal pension** (the part you control).
- Pensions carry **tax breaks** — contributions deductible up to KES 20,000/month; pension income tax-free up to ~KES 300,000/year.
- Aim to save roughly **10–15% of income** for retirement — less if you start early, much more if you delay.
- **Start now, capture free money, use the tax relief, invest for growth while young, and don't raid it.**
