# The Real Cost of Living Alone vs Sharing

Getting your own place feels like the ultimate sign of having made it. But the jump from sharing to living alone is one of the biggest cost increases most young adults ever take on — and it's rarely just "a bit more rent." Before you sign that lease, it's worth seeing the *full* picture: what living alone actually costs versus sharing, and the trade-offs that aren't about money at all. This lesson helps you choose with open eyes, not just emotion.

## Why living alone costs so much more (it's not just rent)

When you share, the big fixed costs are **split**. When you live alone, you carry all of them yourself. The difference shows up across the whole budget:

| Cost | Sharing | Living alone |
|---|---|---|
| **Rent** | Split (a bedsitter shared, or a 2-bed divided) | All yours |
| **Deposit & moving** | Split, or smaller place | Full, up front |
| **Electricity / water** | Split | All yours |
| **Internet** | One bill, split | One bill, all yours |
| **Furnishing** | Often shared/inherited | Buy everything yourself |
| **Food** | Bulk-buying & cooking together is cheaper per person | Cooking for one is pricier per portion |

The hidden one is **furnishing and setup**: living alone means buying *everything* — bed, cooker, utensils, seats — often all at once, which pushes people into expensive hire-purchase or debt (more in Module 5).

A rough rule: living alone can cost **substantially more per month** than sharing the same standard of housing, because every fixed cost that used to be divided is now undivided.

## The upfront wall

Beyond monthly costs, moving out alone has a big **one-time** hit:
- **Deposit** (often one to two months' rent),
- **First month's rent**,
- **Moving costs**,
- **Furnishing the place.**

That can easily total several months' rent before you've spent a single night there. Many people underestimate this and move in financially stressed from day one. The fix from Series 1: a **sinking fund** — save toward the full move-in cost *before* you commit, so you start your new home with a buffer, not a hole.

## The trade-offs beyond money

This isn't purely a numbers decision. Be honest about what you're buying and giving up:

**Living alone gives you:** privacy, full control of your space, quiet, no housemate conflict, freedom on your own schedule. For some people — especially those who need calm to work or study — that's worth paying for.

**Sharing gives you:** much lower cost (the savings can go to your emergency fund or investments), built-in company, shared chores, and someone around in an emergency. The cost is less privacy and the friction of living with others.

There's no universally "right" answer — only what fits your finances and your temperament *right now*. The mistake is treating living alone as an automatic upgrade you must reach for, when sharing for a couple more years might fund your emergency fund, clear debt, or seed an investment.

## A sensible way to decide

- **Run both budgets.** Write out the real monthly cost of each option *plus* the upfront cost. See the gap in shillings.
- **Ask what the difference could do.** If living alone costs, say, KES 12,000 more a month, that's KES 144,000 a year — would that be better as savings or an investment for now?
- **Match it to your life stage.** Sharing early, then living alone once your income and emergency fund are solid, is a common, smart sequence.
- **If you share, agree the money rules upfront** — who pays what, how bills split, what happens if someone's late (see Module 1 on household budgeting).

## Key takeaways

- Living alone means **every fixed cost is undivided** — rent, utilities, internet, furnishing — so it's much pricier than sharing.
- The **upfront move-in cost** (deposit + first rent + moving + furnishing) is often several months' rent; **sink-fund it first**.
- It's a **trade-off, not an upgrade**: privacy and control vs lower cost and company.
- Weigh what the monthly difference could do as **savings or investment** instead.
- A common smart path: **share while you build your base, live alone once your finances are solid.**
