# Real Estate & Land Investing

Real estate is the Kenyan investment dream — "buy land, it never loses value." There's real truth in it: property and land have built enormous wealth here, providing rent, capital growth, and a hedge against inflation. But real estate is also where people tie up huge sums in illiquid, hassle-heavy, sometimes fraud-ridden investments without doing the maths. This lesson looks at property *as an investment* — the realistic returns, the real costs and headaches, and the lower-capital ways in.

> Series 4, Module 7 covers *how to buy land/property safely* (titles, searches, fraud). This lesson is about property as an *investment decision*.

## The two ways property makes money

1. **Rental income** — you let the property and collect rent. The key measure is the **rental yield**: annual rent ÷ property value. In Kenya, residential yields commonly run around **5–8%** (e.g. Kilimani apartments ~6–8%), varying a lot by area and type.
2. **Capital appreciation** — the property/land rises in value over time. Land especially, in growing areas, can appreciate strongly — though it can also stagnate if you buy in the wrong place.

The best property investments deliver **both** — decent rental yield *plus* appreciation. But note: an empty plot of **land** typically gives **no rental income** (you wait purely for appreciation), while a building gives rent but needs management and maintenance.

## The honest downsides (do the maths)

Property's reputation hides real drawbacks that the dream glosses over:
- **It needs big capital.** Buying property usually requires millions, or a mortgage (with interest). This is not a KES 100 starting point.
- **High transaction costs.** Buying costs **7–11%** of the price (stamp duty, legal, etc. — Series 4, Module 7), and selling has costs too. These eat into returns.
- **It's illiquid.** You can't sell quickly if you need cash — a sale can take months. Property is the *opposite* of your emergency fund.
- **It's hands-on.** Rentals mean tenants, repairs, vacancies, rent collection, and management headaches — or paying an agent (cutting your yield).
- **Concentration risk.** One property is a huge chunk of wealth in a single asset in a single location — undiversified.
- **Fraud risk.** Land scams are real and costly (Series 4, Module 7).

Compared honestly: a **money market fund** (~9%, liquid, no hassle, from KES 100) can match or beat many residential rental yields (~5–8%) with none of the headaches. That doesn't make property bad — but it means "property always wins" is a myth. Run the actual numbers.

## REITs: property investing without buying property

Here's the option most Kenyans don't know about. A **REIT (Real Estate Investment Trust)** lets you invest in real estate **without buying a whole property** — you buy units in a trust that owns income-producing property, listed on the NSE and regulated by the **CMA**. Examples in Kenya include **ILAM Fahari** (an income REIT) and **Acorn's** student-housing REITs (Vuka/ASA).

The advantages for an ordinary investor:
- **Far lower capital** — buy in with a fraction of a property's price.
- **Liquidity** — units trade on the NSE, so you can sell more easily than a building.
- **Diversification** — exposure to a portfolio of properties, not one.
- **No management hassle** — professionals run it; you collect distributions.
- **Income** — REITs typically pay out most of their rental income to investors.

REITs are how you get real-estate exposure with the convenience of a share. They carry their own risks (market price moves, the specific REIT's performance), but they're a sensible, accessible route — especially before you have millions for a direct purchase.

## Where property fits in a plan

- **Not your first investment.** Build your emergency fund (MMF), clear bad debt, and have liquid savings *before* tying up large sums in illiquid property.
- **Diversify** — property should be *one* part of a balanced plan (alongside MMF, SACCO, bonds, shares), not your entire net worth.
- **For most beginners, a REIT (or land bought carefully for appreciation) is a more accessible start** than a financed rental.
- **If buying directly,** do the full safety diligence (Series 4, Module 7) and the yield maths — don't buy on the "land never loses" slogan alone.

## Key takeaways

- Property earns through **rental yield (~5–8%)** and **capital appreciation** — the best deliver both; bare land gives no rent.
- The honest downsides: **big capital, 7–11% transaction costs, illiquidity, management hassle, concentration, and fraud risk.**
- An **MMF (~9%, liquid, no hassle)** can rival many rental yields — so "property always wins" is a myth; do the maths.
- **REITs** give real-estate exposure with **low capital, liquidity, diversification, and no hassle** (e.g. ILAM Fahari, Acorn).
- Property is **one part** of a diversified plan, not your first or only investment.
