# Negotiation & Earning More

You can only cut your expenses so far — there's a floor below which you can't go without misery. But there's no ceiling on what you can *earn*. That asymmetry is the most underused truth in personal finance: while budgeting and saving matter enormously, **raising your income is the single biggest lever you have.** And much of that lever comes down to a skill most people never learn — negotiation. This lesson is about earning more, on purpose.

## Why earning more is the bigger lever

Frugality has limits; earning doesn't. Cutting a KES 50,000 budget to the bone might free a few thousand shillings, and it's exhausting to maintain. But raising your income by even 20% — a raise, a better job, a rate increase — adds far more, permanently, and compounds: a higher income means more to save and invest *every month for the rest of your life*. The wealthy focus heavily on growing income, not just trimming costs.

This doesn't make budgeting useless — you still need to *keep* what you earn (otherwise lifestyle inflation eats every raise, Series 3, Module 7). But if you're maxed out on cutting, the next breakthrough is on the **earning** side.

## Know your worth: research the market

You can't negotiate what you can't measure. Before any pay conversation:
- **Find the market rate** for your role, skills and experience. Use Kenyan salary guides (BrighterMonday and Fuzu publish them), job-listing ranges, and — carefully — peers in your field.
- **Know your value, specifically.** What results have you delivered? Numbers persuade: "I grew X by Y," "I saved the team Z." Vague worth gets vague pay.
- **Understand your leverage.** Skills in demand, a strong track record, or another offer all strengthen your hand.

Walking into a negotiation with data turns it from begging into a reasoned business case.

## How to negotiate pay (employed)

Negotiation feels scary, but it's a learnable, normal part of work:
- **Time it right** — at an offer (your peak leverage), at a review, or after a clear win.
- **Let them name a number first** where possible, or **anchor high but reasonable** based on your research — you can come down, but rarely up.
- **Make the case on value, not need.** "Given my results and the market rate, I'm looking for X" beats "I need more because rent went up."
- **Negotiate the whole package** — not just base pay, but allowances, bonus, training, flexibility, title. If pay can't move, other things might.
- **Stay calm and collaborative**, not adversarial. You're solving a problem together, and you may work with these people for years.
- **Be willing to walk** (if you genuinely can) — the strongest position is not needing the yes. Sometimes the best raise is a better job elsewhere.

Remember: **the worst case is usually "no," and you keep what you had.** The downside is small; the upside compounds for years.

## How to raise rates (self-employed / hustle)

If you work for yourself:
- **Price on value delivered**, not just hours or cost-plus.
- **Raise rates with new clients first** (lower risk), then existing ones with notice and justification.
- **Specialise** — niche expertise commands higher rates than generalist work.
- **Improve quality and reliability** so clients happily pay more and refer you.

## Invest in your earning power — the highest return

The surest way to earn more long-term is to become **worth more**. At most life stages, the highest-return "investment" isn't a stock — it's **you**:
- **Skills** that are scarce and in demand (technical, professional, trade).
- **Qualifications and certifications** that open higher-paying doors.
- **A strong reputation and network** — much earning comes through who knows your work.
- **The negotiation skill itself**, which pays back every raise for the rest of your career.

Money spent and time spent raising your earning power compounds harder than almost anything else.

## Key takeaways

- **Earning more is the biggest lever** — cutting costs has a floor; earning has no ceiling, and raises compound for life.
- **Research the market rate** and know your specific value (in numbers) before negotiating.
- Negotiate on **value not need**, anchor on data, negotiate the **whole package**, stay collaborative, and be willing to walk.
- Self-employed: **price on value, raise rates, specialise.**
- The highest-return investment is usually **you** — scarce skills, qualifications, reputation, and the negotiation skill itself.
