# Budgeting as a Family

A personal budget juggles one life. A family budget carries several — children who can't earn, school fees that arrive in lumps, extended family who lean on you, and two adults who must agree. The stakes are higher: a mistake doesn't just inconvenience you, it affects people who depend on you. But a family that runs its money on a shared plan is steadier, argues less, and gives its children both security and a money education by example. This lesson builds that plan.

> This builds on Series 7, Module 1 (running a household budget) and Series 9, Module 1 (money talks with your partner). Here the focus is the family with **dependants**.

## What makes a family budget harder

- **Dependants.** Children consume money and produce none — and their costs *grow* as they do (from nappies to school fees to university).
- **Lumpy, unavoidable costs.** School fees, uniforms, medical bills, and family events don't spread evenly across the month.
- **Black tax.** Real, ongoing support to parents and extended family — noble, but budget-breaking if uncapped.
- **Less margin for error.** With more mouths and fixed obligations, there's less slack when something goes wrong — which makes the emergency fund non-negotiable.

## Step 1 — One honest, combined picture

Bring both incomes and *all* the family's costs into one view (net, take-home income; cautious figures if any income is irregular). The big family categories usually are:
- Housing, food, transport, utilities (the household basics from Series 7).
- **School fees and education** — often the single largest family expense.
- **Children's costs** — uniforms, books, health, activities.
- **Health** — SHIF plus out-of-pocket; children get sick.
- **Family support / black tax.**
- **Debt repayments.**
- **Savings, emergency fund, and the children's-future fund.**

Seeing it all in one place is itself powerful — many couples have never laid the *whole* family picture side by side.

## Step 2 — Tame the lumpy costs with sinking funds

The thing that breaks family budgets isn't the monthly stuff — it's the **big, periodic** hits, above all **school fees**. The fix is the sinking fund: take a known annual cost, divide by 12, and save that slice every month so the cost is already waiting when it lands.

> **School fees example.** Fees of about KES 30,000 per term × 3 terms = KES 90,000 a year. Save `90,000 ÷ 12 =` **KES 7,500 a month** into a separate pot. When the term opens, the money is there — no panic loan, no borrowing at brutal rates.

Do the same for uniforms/back-to-school, the festive season, and known annual events. A family that sinks-funds its lumpy costs almost never needs emergency debt — which is where so many families quietly lose money to interest.

## Step 3 — Put a number on black tax

Family support is part of life here, and often the right thing to do. The danger is when it has **no limit** — every request met from whatever's left, until the family's own plan collapses. The healthier approach:
- Decide *together*, in advance, a **fixed monthly amount** the family can sustainably give.
- Treat it as a budget line, like any other — generous *and* bounded.
- Where possible, help family toward **self-reliance** (a one-off that builds income) rather than endless monthly top-ups.

This protects your own children's security while still honouring family — and removes a major source of couple conflict.

## Step 4 — Protect the family and involve it

- **Emergency fund first.** With dependants, aim toward the larger end (closer to 6 months of costs) because more people rely on you. It's the buffer that stops one shock becoming a disaster.
- **Insure the breadwinners.** With people depending on your income, life and health cover move from "nice to have" to essential (Series 9, Module 6).
- **Involve the family.** Age-appropriately, let children see budgeting happen — choosing, saving, waiting. You're not just managing money; you're raising money-smart adults (Module 4). A shared family goal ("we're saving for X") turns budgeting into teamwork.

## A simple family budget shape

For a family with **KES 100,000** combined take-home, the *shape* often looks like:

| Category | Amount (KES) |
|---|---|
| Housing | 22,000 |
| Food | 24,000 |
| School-fees sinking fund | 8,000 |
| Transport | 9,000 |
| Utilities | 7,000 |
| Children's costs (uniforms, health, activities) | 6,000 |
| Family support / black tax (capped) | 6,000 |
| Health (SHIF + out-of-pocket) | 4,000 |
| Savings / emergency / children's future | 10,000 |
| Personal money (split) | 4,000 |
| **Total** | **100,000** |

Illustrative only — your real numbers depend on your town, school, and family size. The principle holds: basics first, lumpy costs pre-funded, black tax capped, savings a fixed line.

## Key takeaways

- A family budget carries **dependants and lumpy costs** — higher stakes, less margin for error.
- Build **one honest, combined** picture of all income and all family costs.
- **Sink-fund school fees and big costs** so they never trigger panic loans.
- **Cap black tax** to a sustainable monthly figure decided together.
- With dependants, the **emergency fund and breadwinner insurance** are essential, and **involving the family** teaches the next generation.
