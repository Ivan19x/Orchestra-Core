# How the Wealthy Think About Money

Two people earn KES 120,000 a month. One drives a sleek financed car, lives in a pricey apartment, and is one missed payday from disaster. The other drives a modest second-hand car, owns a rental plot upcountry, and quietly grows richer every month. From the outside, the first looks wealthy. In reality, the second *is*.

The difference is not income. It is how they think. This lesson is about that mindset — the handful of mental models that financially successful people use, stripped of the hype.

## The one idea that changes everything: assets vs liabilities

Forget the dictionary for a moment and use the practical, wealth-building definition:

- An **asset** puts money *into* your pocket.
- A **liability** takes money *out* of your pocket.

That is the whole game. Wealthy people spend their lives **buying assets**; everyone else spends their life buying liabilities and calling them assets.

Kenyan examples make it concrete:

| Often called an "asset" | Reality |
|---|---|
| A financed car for personal use | **Liability** — fuel, insurance, service, and it loses value every year |
| A rental plot or *bedsitter* you let out | **Asset** — pays you rent monthly |
| The latest phone on a payment plan | **Liability** — drains money, drops in value |
| SACCO deposits / shares earning dividends | **Asset** — pays you dividends and rebates |
| Money in a money market fund | **Asset** — earns interest |
| A loan for a wedding or holiday | **Liability** — costs you for years, builds nothing |

Note the trap: the *same thing* can be either. A car you use for a delivery business that earns more than it costs is an asset. The identical car bought purely for image is a liability. It is not the object — it is whether money flows in or out.

## Making money work for you

Most people trade time for money: you work, you get paid, you stop working, the money stops. The wealthy add a second engine — **money that earns money** — so income arrives even when they are asleep. In Kenya that engine is built from ordinary, accessible things:

- **SACCO** share capital that pays annual dividends.
- A **money market fund** that pays interest daily.
- **Government bonds** (via DhowCSD) that pay interest twice a year.
- A small **rental** unit that pays monthly.

You do not need millions to start. The mindset shift is to see every spare shilling as a potential little worker you can hire — and to buy assets *before* luxuries, not after.

## Looking rich vs being wealthy

This is the deepest idea, and the hardest in a society that prizes display. Research on real millionaires (famously in *The Millionaire Next Door*) found that genuinely wealthy people are usually *less* flashy than people assume — modest cars, modest homes, quiet habits. The flashy ones are often the most fragile, because the lifestyle *is* the spending.

- **Looking rich** = high spending, visible things, often funded by debt.
- **Being wealthy** = high *net worth* — what you'd have left if you sold everything and cleared every debt.

You cannot see someone's net worth from their Instagram. The KES 120,000 earner with the rental plot is winning, even though the financed-car earner looks like they are.

## The long-term mindset

Wealthy thinking runs on patience and compounding, not quick wins:
- **Time in the market beats timing it.** Small, consistent investing over years beats clever bets.
- **Avoid wealth-destroyers:** betting, "get-rich-quick" schemes, and bad debt undo years of saving in weeks.
- **Live below your means and bank the gap.** The size of the gap between what you earn and what you spend — not your salary — is what builds wealth.

## Key takeaways

- **Assets put money in your pocket; liabilities take it out.** Spend your life buying assets.
- The same object can be an asset or a liability — it depends on whether money flows in or out.
- Build a second income engine (SACCO, MMF, bonds, rentals) so money earns money.
- **Net worth, not display, is wealth.** Looking rich and being wealthy are often opposites.
- Wealth is patient: consistency and compounding beat quick wins.
